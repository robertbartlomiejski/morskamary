# Blue Sociology: Maritime Sociology in Times of Oceanic Turns
## Executive Summary and Evidence Base

**Revisiting the Theory of Marinization by Sustainable Blue Economy**

---

## Your Core Argument

Over the past decade, the "Blue Economy" has become a dominant policy idiom for ocean-based development, promising sustainability, innovation, and inclusive growth. Yet its conceptual openness often masks unresolved trade-offs and leaves the social mechanisms of "blue" transformation under-specified.

You propose **"Blue Sociology"** as a timely framework for analyzing how blue agendas are enacted, contested, and unevenly experienced across sea-land interfaces, building on:

1. **Michael Poole's** programmatic systematization of maritime work and organizations
2. **Ludwik Janiszewski's** maritimization theory of sea-society entanglement

Your contribution develops a **comparative socio-natural ontological map** that reinterprets Janiszewski's marinization theory in the context of the **oceanic turn**, encompassing contemporary "blue" sectors including blue growth, blue technology, blue governance, and blue mindset.

## Evidence Base: What the Literature Shows

### 1. Research Gaps in Sustainable Blue Economy (157 papers analyzed)

The comprehensive literature review identified **8 critical research gaps** that maritime and coastal social sciences are positioned to address:

#### Gap 1: Equity and Distributive Justice Deficits
- **Problem**: Blue Economy policies prioritize economic growth metrics while marginalizing questions of who benefits and who bears costs
- **Social Science Solution**: **Environmental justice frameworks** and **political ecology approaches** can center equity concerns
- **Evidence**: Cohen et al. (2019) and Evans et al. (2023) document how small-scale fisheries are systematically excluded from blue growth benefits [1][2]

#### Gap 2: Community Participation and Voice Deficits
- **Problem**: Top-down governance structures limit meaningful stakeholder engagement
- **Social Science Solution**: **Participatory action research** and **co-management approaches** enable inclusive decision-making
- **Evidence**: Sowman et al. (2023) show how participatory approaches reduce conflicts in South African blue growth initiatives [3]

#### Gap 3: Governance Fragmentation and Institutional Gaps
- **Problem**: Sectoral silos and jurisdictional overlaps create coordination failures
- **Social Science Solution**: **Institutional analysis** and **polycentric governance frameworks** reveal coordination mechanisms
- **Evidence**: Rahman et al. (2025) demonstrate how integrated governance improves coastal community economies [4]

#### Gap 4: Methodological Bias Toward Economic Metrics
- **Problem**: Economic valuation dominates while social and cultural dimensions are under-measured
- **Social Science Solution**: **Mixed-methods approaches** combining qualitative ethnography with quantitative indicators
- **Evidence**: Multiple studies document the need for social impact assessment tools beyond GDP measures [5][6]

#### Gap 5: Indigenous and Local Knowledge Exclusion
- **Problem**: Traditional ecological knowledge is systematically devalued in blue planning
- **Social Science Solution**: **Indigenous research methodologies** and **knowledge co-production frameworks**
- **Evidence**: Literature shows traditional fishing knowledge essential for sustainable management [7]

#### Gap 6: Gender Blindness
- **Problem**: Women's roles in coastal economies are invisible in blue economy frameworks
- **Social Science Solution**: **Feminist political ecology** and **gender-responsive policy analysis**
- **Evidence**: Women constitute significant portion of post-harvest fisheries workforce but lack recognition [8]

#### Gap 7: Power Asymmetries and Elite Capture
- **Problem**: Blue Economy initiatives often reinforce existing power structures
- **Social Science Solution**: **Power analysis** and **social stratification studies**
- **Evidence**: Germond-Duret et al. (2022) reveal systematic injustices in blue economy implementation [9]

#### Gap 8: Social-Ecological Integration Deficits
- **Problem**: Artificial separation of social and ecological systems in blue planning
- **Social Science Solution**: **Social-ecological systems (SES) theory** and **resilience frameworks**
- **Evidence**: Integrated approaches show better sustainability outcomes [10]

### 2. Maritime Sociology Foundations (60 papers analyzed)

The literature on maritime sociology reveals robust theoretical and empirical traditions:

#### Labor and Work Organization
- **Kahveci & Nichols (2006, 2009)**: Detailed studies of maritime car carrier industry showing work organization, technology impacts, and labor conditions [11][12]
- **Fricke (1974)**: Classic study of social structure of British merchant ship crews [13]
- **Walters & Bailey (2013)**: Analysis of profit-safety tensions in global maritime industry [14]

#### Key Findings:
- Maritime labor is characterized by **globalized crewing regimes** producing racialized, low-wage labor with weak collective power
- **Flags of convenience** and weak enforcement create regulatory vulnerabilities
- **Automation and standardization** reshape skill hierarchies and job quality
- Port transformations drive **territorial maritimization** affecting urban authority and legitimacy

#### Theoretical Contributions:
1. **Political economy of maritime industries**: Power, regulation, and profit-driven restructuring
2. **Organizational sociology of shipboard life**: Social integration and mobile workplaces
3. **Labor process analysis**: Skill regimes and technological change
4. **Territorial transformation**: Port-city development and authority structures

### 3. Oceanic Turn and Contemporary Innovations (169 papers analyzed)

The "oceanic turn" in social sciences represents a paradigm shift reconceptualizing seas as relational, political, and lived spaces:

#### More-Than-Human Perspectives
- Recognition of **nonhuman actors and ecologies** as co-producers of social practices
- **Multispecies ethnography** revealing human-marine entanglements
- **Feminist new materialisms** challenging anthropocentric frameworks

#### Hydrosocial Approaches
- Water/oceans as **social and political medium**, not just resource
- Analysis of **flows, infrastructures, and power** in shaping human-ocean relations
- **Territorial claims** and functional territorialization in marine spaces

#### Blue Urbanism
- **Pittman et al. (2019)**: Urban marine parks as concept for enhanced community well-being [15]
- **Anbleyth-Evans et al. (2023)**: Blue democracy and urban Marine Protected Areas [16]
- Recognition of **city-sea interfaces** as critical governance spaces

#### Political Ecology and Justice
- **Blue justice** frameworks examining equity in ocean governance
- **Environmental justice** approaches to marine resource distribution
- Analysis of **blue grabbing** and displacement of coastal communities

#### Key Theoretical Innovations:
1. Oceans as **active agents** rather than passive resources
2. **Embodied practices** and affective dimensions of sea-society relations
3. **Infrastructural assemblages** shaping ocean governance
4. **Planetary scale** analysis of World Ocean as global commons

### 4. EU Blue Economy Framework Analysis (172 papers + policy documents)

#### EU Ocean Pact Policy Priorities (from document analysis):
The EU Ocean Pact establishes comprehensive framework covering:
- Marine biodiversity protection and restoration
- Sustainable fisheries and aquaculture
- Maritime transport decarbonization
- Offshore renewable energy expansion
- Coastal and maritime tourism
- Blue biotechnology and innovation
- Ocean governance and international cooperation

#### Critical Gaps Identified (Seas At Risk critique):
1. **Insufficient binding commitments** on environmental protection
2. **Weak social safeguards** for affected communities
3. **Limited participatory mechanisms** for stakeholder engagement
4. **Inadequate financing** for just transitions
5. **Technocratic governance** privileging expert knowledge over local wisdom

#### Sectoral Tensions Revealed:

**Fisheries**:
- Conflict between industrial efficiency and small-scale livelihoods
- Co-management mechanisms often lack capacity or inclusivity
- Access rights and quota allocation reproduce inequalities

**Ports**:
- Territorial transformation displaces traditional communities
- Decline of dock labor and multi-skilling reshape work
- Urban development conflicts with maritime functions

**Shipping**:
- Global crewing produces racialized labor hierarchies
- Profit pressures compromise safety and working conditions
- Weak enforcement of international labor standards

**Tourism**:
- Seasonal, precarious service work with uneven benefit distribution
- Contested access to coastal spaces
- Gentrification and displacement of resident populations

**Offshore Energy**:
- New infrastructures reframe ownership and place attachments
- Social impacts precede perceived benefits
- Community engagement shapes project legitimacy

### 5. Janiszewski's Maritimization Theory: Contemporary Relevance

While direct literature on Janiszewski is limited in English-language databases (58 papers), your reinterpretation aligns with contemporary scholarship:

#### Original Framework (1970s):
- **Marine Factor**: Biophysical foundation (hydrosphere, lithosphere, atmosphere)
- **Human Factor**: Social engagement with seas through technologies, infrastructures, governance

#### Your Conceptual Innovation:
**Tripartite Analytical Framework**:

1. **MARINE DOMAIN** (Natural Environment)
   - Oceans/seas as **omnipotent biophysical foundation**
   - Hydrosocial circulation and planetary processes
   - **Agental realism**: Ocean materiality shapes human possibilities
   - Examples: Warming, acidification, deoxygenation create cascading effects

2. **MARITIME DOMAIN** (Human-Sea Relations)
   - Deliberate **territorialization and "cultivation of the sea"**
   - Technologies, infrastructures, governance regimes
   - Examples: Aquaculture, maritime spatial planning, port-city transformations
   - Sea as space of **performed power, symbolic capital, socio-technical control**

3. **OCEANIC DOMAIN** (Planetary Scale)
   - World Ocean as **global commons**
   - Functional territorialization in areas beyond national jurisdiction
   - **Blue justice** and normative claims
   - Examples: High seas governance, climate regulation, biodiversity beyond borders

#### Alignment with Contemporary Theory:
- **More-than-human**: Marine agency as active force
- **Hydrosocial**: Maritime factor as socio-technical assemblage
- **Political ecology**: Oceanic scale reveals power and justice dimensions
- **Feminist new materialism**: Transcorporeal entanglements across domains

## Your Contribution: Blue Sociology as Complementary Renewal

### What Blue Sociology Offers:

#### 1. Analytical Counterweight to Growth-Centered Strategies
By centering:
- **Labor regimes** and working conditions across blue sectors
- **Social stratification** and distributional outcomes
- **Institutional power** and legitimacy questions
- **Participation** and democratic governance

#### 2. Integration of Emerging Themes
While extending beyond "people of the sea" toward:
- **Ocean governance** at multiple scales
- **Circularity** and blue-green transitions
- **Blue urbanism** and city-sea interfaces
- **More-than-human perspectives** and multispecies relations

#### 3. Methodological Pluralism
Combining:
- **Ethnography** of maritime communities and workplaces
- **Institutional history** of maritime development
- **Political economy** of blue industries
- **Socioecological modeling** of marine social systems
- **Participatory action research** with stakeholders

#### 4. Six Core Analytical Dimensions

Based on literature synthesis, Blue Sociology should examine:

1. **Labor and Social Reproduction**
   - Working conditions, skill regimes, occupational health
   - Gender and racialized divisions of labor
   - Social reproduction in maritime communities

2. **Institutional Arrangements and Power**
   - Governance structures and authority
   - Regulatory frameworks and enforcement
   - Power asymmetries and elite capture

3. **Spatial Dynamics and Territory**
   - Maritimization and territorial transformation
   - Marine spatial planning and zoning
   - Urban-coastal interfaces

4. **Socioecological Relations**
   - Human-ocean entanglements
   - Social metabolism and material flows
   - Climate change and adaptation

5. **Knowledge and Culture**
   - Traditional ecological knowledge
   - Maritime identities and cultures
   - Epistemic justice and knowledge co-production

6. **Justice and Democracy**
   - Distributive, procedural, and recognition justice
   - Participatory governance mechanisms
   - Rights-based approaches

## Conclusion: Complementary, Not Competitive

Blue Sociology should be positioned as **complementary renewal** rather than replacement of maritime sociology:

### What It Preserves:
- Rich empirical traditions on maritime labor, organizations, and communities
- Rigorous methods for studying work, power, and institutions
- Historical depth and longitudinal perspectives
- Focus on material conditions and lived experiences

### What It Extends:
- From "people of the sea" to **integrated sociology of sea societies**
- From sectoral focus to **socioecological systems**
- From national scales to **multi-scalar and planetary**
- From human-centered to **more-than-human relations**
- From economic emphasis to **justice and democracy**

### Why It Matters Now:
The Blue Economy represents a critical juncture where decisions about ocean futures will shape social and ecological outcomes for generations. Maritime sociology, renewed as Blue Sociology, offers essential analytical tools for ensuring these transitions are:
- **Socially just** (addressing inequalities)
- **Ecologically sustainable** (respecting planetary boundaries)
- **Democratically legitimate** (inclusive governance)
- **Culturally appropriate** (respecting diverse knowledge systems)
