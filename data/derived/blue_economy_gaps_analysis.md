## TL;DR

Social science reveals recurring gaps in blue economy research: justice and equity are sidelined, participation is weak, governance is fragmented, economic metrics dominate, Indigenous and gendered knowledges are excluded, power asymmetries persist, and social–ecological links are poorly integrated. Targeted maritime social science methods can redress each problem.

----

## Equity and justice gaps

Coastal and maritime research often treats blue economy growth as value-neutral, which sidelines distributive and recognitional justice for small-scale fishers and coastal communities. Addressing these deficits requires frameworks that expose structural drivers of marginalization and reframe policy priorities toward fairness.

- **Gap description**  
  - Many blue economy policies and analyses underplay social justice, enabling dispossession, marginalisation of small-scale fisheries, and exclusion from decision-making processes [1] [2].  
- **How social science can help**  
  - Political ecology and blue justice frameworks surface historical, economic, and institutional drivers of inequity and reframe analyses to centre rights, livelihoods, and food security [2] [3].  
- **Specific potential**  
  - Political ecology frameworks and blue justice approaches.  
- **Why this approach works**  
  - These frameworks link macro-level political economy with local experiences, making visible processes (industrialisation, spatial enclosure) that produce inequity and thus informing redistributive and recognition-oriented policy interventions [2] [4].  
- **Key references**  
  - Louisa Evans, P. M. Buchan, Matt Fortnam, M. B. Honig, Louise Heaps, Putting coastal communities at the center of a sustainable blue economy: A review of risks, opportunities, and strategies, Frontiers in Political Science, 2023, doi:10.3389/fpos.2022.1032204 [1].  
  - Philippa J. Cohen, Edward H. Allison, Neil L. Andrew et al., Securing a Just Space for Small-Scale Fisheries in the Blue Economy, Frontiers in Marine Science, 2019, doi:10.3389/fmars.2019.00171 [2].

## Participation and inclusion deficits

Empirical studies document recurrent failures to meaningfully consult or co‑design with coastal communities, producing conflict and local resistance to blue projects. Strengthening deliberative and participatory practices can reduce conflict and improve legitimacy.

- **Gap description**  
  - Rapid promotion of blue growth frequently fast‑tracks planning and investment while marginalising affected communities, producing conflicts and weak social legitimacy for projects [5] [6].  
- **How social science can help**  
  - Deliberative, participatory and co‑production approaches from maritime social science can open inclusive spaces for knowledge exchange and collective decision-making, shifting processes away from top‑down rollouts [5] [6].  
- **Specific potential**  
  - Participatory action research, co‑production of knowledge, stakeholder-driven scenario building.  
- **Why this approach works**  
  - Participatory methods build trust, surface local priorities and values, and produce actionable local buy‑in; they can also reveal trade‑offs and alternatives early, reducing later conflict and distributional harms [5].  
- **Key references**  
  - Merle Sowman, Philile Mbatha, Johanna von Holdt, Strategies for addressing conflicts arising from blue growth initiatives: insights from three case studies in South Africa, Maritime Studies, 2023, doi:10.1007/s40152-023-00341-1 [5].  
  - Nathan J. Bennett, Navigating a just and inclusive path towards sustainable oceans, Marine Policy, 2018, doi:10.1016/J.MARPOL.2018.06.001 [6].

## Governance fragmentation and power asymmetries

Blue economy governance charts often ignore multi‑scalar interactions and entrenched power imbalances, producing scale mismatches and privileging investors over local rights. Critical governance analyses and place-based methods can reveal and rebalance these dynamics.

- **Gap description**  
  - Governance arrangements across sectors and scales are fragmented; power asymmetries favour industry and state actors and constrain small‑scale users, producing exclusion and ocean‑grabbing dynamics [7] [3].  
- **How social science can help**  
  - Multi‑level governance analysis, political ecology and place‑based research highlight where institutional fragmentation and power concentrations undermine equitable outcomes and identify loci for reform [7] [8].  
- **Specific potential**  
  - Political ecology frameworks, multi‑scalar governance analysis, place‑based participatory mapping.  
- **Why this approach works**  
  - These methods trace institutions and power relations across scales, enabling targeted governance reforms (e.g., co‑management, fiscal measures, rights recognition) that reallocate authority and benefits toward marginalized actors [7] [8].  
- **Key references**  
  - Pedro Fidelman, Louisa Evans, Michael Fabinyi, Simon Foale, Josh E. Cinner, Governing large-scale marine commons: contextual challenges in the Coral Triangle, Marine Policy, 2012, doi:10.1016/J.MARPOL.2011.03.007 [7].  
  - Jack L. Harris, Benjamin S. Thompson, Supporting places left to the sea: A place-based research agenda for regional coastal transformations in the blue economy, Progress in Environmental Geography, 2023, doi:10.1177/27539687231212672 [8].

## Methodological bias toward economic metrics

Blue economy research and policy evaluation often foreground GDP and sectoral growth metrics, marginalizing social, cultural and ecological values. Integrative social‑ecological and qualitative methods can rebalance metrics and evidence used for decision-making.

- **Gap description**  
  - Economic indicators dominate assessments of blue initiatives, underrepresenting wellbeing, food security, cultural values and cumulative impacts on vulnerable communities [1] [6].  
- **How social science can help**  
  - Social‑ecological systems theory, mixed‑methods evaluation, and social impact assessment can incorporate non‑market values, distributional outcomes and long‑term resilience into appraisal frameworks [1] [6].  
- **Specific potential**  
  - Social‑ecological systems theory, mixed‑methods impact evaluation, participatory indicators.  
- **Why this approach works**  
  - SES theory links social and ecological dynamics and supports indicators that reflect ecosystem services, livelihoods, and equity rather than only monetary outputs, enabling more holistic and precautionary policy choices [1] [6].  
- **Key references**  
  - Louisa Evans, P. M. Buchan, Matt Fortnam, M. B. Honig, Louise Heaps, Putting coastal communities at the center of a sustainable blue economy: A review of risks, opportunities, and strategies, Frontiers in Political Science, 2023, doi:10.3389/fpos.2022.1032204 [1].  
  - Nathan J. Bennett, Navigating a just and inclusive path towards sustainable oceans, Marine Policy, 2018, doi:10.1016/J.MARPOL.2018.06.001 [6].

## Indigenous and local knowledge exclusion and gender blindness

Indigenous, local and gendered knowledges are frequently marginalised in blue economy agendas, eroding culturally embedded management systems and obscuring gendered impacts. Feminist and decolonial methodologies plus ethnographic engagement can restore these knowledges to policy design.

- **Gap description**  
  - Planning rarely integrates Indigenous and local knowledge or examines gendered roles and impacts, resulting in lost stewardship practices and disproportionate burdens on women and marginalized groups [9] [4].  
- **How social science can help**  
  - Ethnography, feminist political ecology, and Indigenous knowledge integration enable documentation of local practices, gendered labour, and place‑based governance that are essential for equitable and adaptive blue policies [9] [4].  
- **Specific potential**  
  - Ethnographic methods, feminist political ecology, Indigenous knowledge integration, participatory mapping.  
- **Why this approach works**  
  - These approaches surface lived experience, gendered divisions of labour and non‑market values, and can be operationalised in co‑management or rights‑based policies that protect cultural practices and ensure benefit sharing [9] [4].  
- **Key references**  
  - Leopoldo Cavaleri Gerhardinger, Elyse Mills, Beatriz Mesquita, Vivienne Solis Rivera, Henrique Calori Kefalás, Challenging the blue economy: voices from artisanal fishing communities in Latin America and the Caribbean, Development, 2023, doi:10.1057/s41301-023-00366-3 [9].  
  - R. K. Ayilu, Michael Fabinyi, Kate Barclay, Mary Ama Bawa, Blue economy: industrialisation and coastal fishing livelihoods in Ghana, Reviews in Fish Biology and Fisheries, 2023, doi:10.1007/s11160-022-09749-0 [4].

## Power asymmetries and social license risks

Research shows industry‑led ocean development can secure formal permissions while failing to secure social legitimacy, escalating conflict and rights abuses. Social science methods that probe power and SLO dynamics can inform more accountable development pathways.

- **Gap description**  
  - Projects may achieve regulatory approval while lacking social licence to operate, reflecting unequal negotiation power and limited mechanisms to address community grievances [10] [5].  
- **How social science can help**  
  - Critical studies of social licence, advocacy research and conflict analysis expose asymmetries in negotiation, enabling design of governance mechanisms that mandate consent, transparency and accountability [10] [5].  
- **Specific potential**  
  - Social licence to operate analysis, conflict mapping, advocacy and legal‑empowerment research.  
- **Why this approach works**  
  - SLO research documents legitimacy gaps and offers procedural remedies (early engagement, transparent benefit sharing, grievance redress) that reduce contestation and protect vulnerable stakeholders [10] [5].  
- **Key references**  
  - Michelle Voyer, Judith van Leeuwen, ‘Social license to operate’ in the Blue Economy, Resources Policy, 2019, doi:10.1016/J.RESOURPOL.2019.02.020 [10].  
  - Merle Sowman, Philile Mbatha, Johanna von Holdt, Strategies for addressing conflicts arising from blue growth initiatives: insights from three case studies in South Africa, Maritime Studies, 2023, doi:10.1007/s40152-023-00341-1 [5].
