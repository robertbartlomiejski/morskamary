# Academic Analysis of EU Ocean Pact Documents: Policy Priorities, Critical Gaps, and Social Dimensions

## Executive Summary

This analysis examines the European Ocean Pact and its critical assessment by environmental NGO Seas At Risk. Based on available documentation, this research identifies key policy priorities, governance mechanisms, and critically examines how social dimensions are framed (or omitted) within EU ocean governance. The analysis reveals significant tensions between economic growth imperatives and social sustainability goals, while highlighting institutional limitations from a social science perspective.

**Note on Sources**: This analysis is primarily based on the official EU Ocean Pact document ("Pakt na rzecz wszechoceanu"). The Seas At Risk critique document encountered technical accessibility issues during processing. Future research should incorporate this critical civil society perspective for a more complete analysis.

## 1. Main Policy Priorities and Frameworks in the EU Ocean Pact

The European Ocean Pact establishes a comprehensive, multi-sectoral approach to ocean governance, positioning the EU as a global leader in marine policy. The framework recognizes oceans as critical for "life on Earth, prosperity, competitiveness, security, and a sustainable future" while acknowledging severe degradation from climate change, pollution, and overexploitation.

### 1.1 Core Policy Priorities

**Protection and Restoration of Ocean Health**
The pact prioritizes safeguarding and restoring ocean health, including marine biological resources and biodiversity. This involves addressing pollution from both terrestrial and marine sources—plastics, chemical runoff—and promoting sustainable fishing practices [1][2][3]. This priority reflects the EU's commitment to ecosystem-based management approaches.

**Boosting Sustainable Blue Economy Competitiveness**
A central framework objective is fostering "a competitive and sustainable blue economy," encompassing both traditional sectors (fisheries, aquaculture) and emerging industries (marine renewable energy, blue biotechnology) [1][4]. The emphasis on "competitiveness" alongside sustainability reveals inherent tensions explored further in Section 4.

**Supporting Coastal and Island Communities**
The pact aims to "enhance the resilience and economic autonomy of coastal and island communities," particularly in outermost regions, through sustainable tourism and new business models that ostensibly benefit "both nature and people" [5][6][7]. This priority gestures toward social equity but lacks specificity regarding mechanisms for ensuring distributive justice.

**Advancing Ocean Research and Knowledge**
The framework emphasizes research, knowledge development, skills, and innovation, including "an ambitious ocean observation initiative" and strengthened scientific cooperation [5][8][9]. This priority privileges techno-scientific approaches to ocean governance.

**Enhancing Maritime Security and Defense**
Responding to geopolitical tensions, the pact addresses threats to underwater infrastructure, cybersecurity, and freedom of navigation through improved maritime security capabilities and naval cooperation [5][10]. This securitization of ocean space has implications for access and governance discussed below.

**Strengthening EU Ocean Diplomacy and International Governance**
The EU commits to upholding UNCLOS (United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea), ratifying the BBNJ (Biodiversity Beyond National Jurisdiction) agreement, combating IUU (illegal, unreported, and unregulated) fishing, and advocating for global treaties on marine pollution [5][11][12].

### 1.2 Institutional Framework

The pact proposes a "unified reference framework" to improve coordination, simplify reporting, and ensure coherent implementation across sectors, including an "Ocean Act" based on reviewing the Marine Spatial Planning Directive [13]. This technocratic approach to governance integration raises questions about democratic participation and accountability.

## 2. Critical Gaps and Missing Dimensions

### 2.1 Limitation of Available Critique

**Important Methodological Note**: The Seas At Risk critique document, which would provide systematic civil society assessment of gaps and tensions in the Ocean Pact, was not accessible for this analysis due to technical processing issues. This represents a significant limitation, as NGO critiques often identify blind spots in official policy documents, particularly regarding:
- Environmental protection adequacy
- Greenwashing concerns
- Industry capture of policy processes
- Insufficient binding commitments
- Weak enforcement mechanisms

Future research must incorporate this critical perspective to provide a complete analysis of contested terrain in EU ocean governance.

### 2.2 Gaps Evident from Document Analysis

Even without the Seas At Risk critique, several critical gaps emerge from analyzing the Ocean Pact itself:

**Absence of Rights-Based Frameworks**
The document lacks explicit rights-based language regarding ocean access, marine resources, or coastal livelihoods. There is no mention of "rights" for fishing communities, coastal populations, or future generations, despite rhetoric about "solidarity" and "justice."

**Limited Binding Commitments**
The pact primarily uses aspirational language ("aims to," "seeks to," "will support") rather than establishing binding commitments with clear accountability mechanisms and enforcement provisions.

**Weak Integration of Social Impact Assessment**
While the document mentions social inclusion, it provides no framework for systematically assessing social impacts of ocean policies or for ensuring that economic development genuinely benefits marginalized coastal communities.

**Insufficient Attention to Power Asymmetries**
The pact fails to address power imbalances between large-scale industrial actors (shipping, offshore energy, industrial fishing) and small-scale, artisanal, or traditional users of marine spaces.

## 3. Framing of Social Dimensions: A Critical Analysis

This section examines how the Ocean Pact frames—or conspicuously fails to frame—critical social dimensions of ocean governance. The analysis reveals a pattern of superficial acknowledgment without substantive integration of social justice concerns.

### 3.1 Labor and Working Conditions

**Limited Treatment**: The pact notes that the blue economy "directly provides almost 5 million jobs and contributes over 250 billion EUR in gross value added annually" [20], framing labor primarily through economic contribution metrics rather than worker rights or decent work frameworks.

**Gaps in Labor Analysis**:
- No mention of labor rights, working conditions, or occupational safety in maritime industries
- Absence of discussion regarding precarious employment in fishing, aquaculture, or maritime transport
- No reference to ILO (International Labour Organization) conventions for maritime workers
- Missing analysis of labor impacts from automation and technological change in maritime sectors
- No consideration of migrant labor issues in fishing and seafood processing industries

The pact mentions "improving working conditions" in the fishing sector [6] but provides no specific mechanisms, standards, or accountability measures. This superficial treatment suggests labor is viewed instrumentally—as a factor of production—rather than as a domain requiring rights-based protections.

### 3.2 Community Participation and Participatory Governance

**Rhetoric vs. Mechanisms**: The document states the pact "seeks to involve all relevant stakeholders, including fishers, blue economy specialists, innovators, investors, scientists, and civil society" [13][16]. However, this stakeholder list reveals a particular framing:

**Critical Observations**:
- "Fishers" are mentioned, but not fishing communities, coastal populations, or marine-dependent societies
- "Innovators" and "investors" are explicitly named, suggesting prioritization of capital and entrepreneurship
- "Civil society" is mentioned generically without specifying environmental NGOs, community organizations, or social movements
- No mention of participatory mechanisms, decision-making power, or co-management arrangements
- Absence of free, prior, and informed consent (FPIC) principles for communities affected by marine development projects

**Governance Structure**: The proposed "high-level Ocean Council" comprises "representatives from relevant ocean-related sectors" [14] but lacks specification of how civil society, small-scale fishers, or coastal communities will be represented. This suggests a corporatist governance model favoring organized sectoral interests over grassroots participation.

### 3.3 Distributive Justice and Equity

**Weak Framing**: The pact emphasizes "social inclusion, justice, and solidarity" [16] in coordinating maritime activities, but these terms remain undefined and unoperationalized. 

**Missing Dimensions**:
- No framework for assessing distributional impacts of ocean policies across social groups, regions, or generations
- Absence of mechanisms ensuring equitable benefit-sharing from marine resources, particularly genetic resources and minerals mentioned as economic opportunities [6]
- No discussion of how revenues from expanding blue economy sectors will be distributed
- Missing analysis of who bears costs vs. who captures benefits from marine conservation measures
- No consideration of spatial justice—how marine spatial planning affects different users' access to ocean space

**Outermost Regions**: The pact mentions supporting "outermost regions" [5][6][7], acknowledging geographical peripherality, but provides no specific framework for addressing structural inequalities or ensuring these regions benefit equitably from ocean governance reforms.

### 3.4 Traditional and Indigenous Knowledge

**Conspicuous Absence**: This represents perhaps the most glaring gap in the Ocean Pact's social framing.

**What's Missing**:
- No explicit mention of "traditional knowledge," "indigenous knowledge," or "local ecological knowledge"
- Absence of recognition of traditional fishing practices, customary marine tenure, or ancestral connections to marine spaces
- No reference to indigenous peoples' rights in marine contexts (despite EU commitments to indigenous rights in other policy domains)
- Missing integration of traditional knowledge systems in ocean observation, monitoring, or management
- No acknowledgment of cultural dimensions of human-ocean relationships beyond economic and recreational uses

**Epistemic Privileging**: The pact's emphasis on "science-based decision-making" [implied throughout] and "ocean observation initiative" [5][8][9] privileges Western scientific knowledge systems without acknowledging complementary knowledge systems developed through generations of coastal and maritime livelihoods.

This omission is particularly problematic given:
- Growing recognition in international environmental governance of traditional knowledge value
- The BBNJ agreement's provisions on indigenous and local knowledge (which the pact commits to ratifying)
- Evidence that traditional management practices often align with sustainability objectives
- EU's own commitments to cultural diversity and minority rights

### 3.5 Gender Dimensions

**Minimal Treatment**: The pact includes one sentence: "Particular emphasis will be placed on strengthening the position of women and other underrepresented groups to ensure their active and significant role in ocean-related activities" [9].

**Critical Assessment**:
- This represents tokenistic acknowledgment without substantive gender analysis
- No recognition of gendered divisions of labor in maritime sectors (e.g., women's predominance in seafood processing, men's in fishing)
- Absence of gender-disaggregated data or gender impact assessment frameworks
- No discussion of how ocean policies differentially affect women and men
- Missing analysis of women's exclusion from decision-making in traditionally male-dominated maritime sectors
- No consideration of intersectionality—how gender intersects with class, race, ethnicity, or geography in shaping ocean-related opportunities and vulnerabilities

**Framing Issue**: Women are framed as "underrepresented groups" needing "strengthened position"—a deficit model that locates the problem in women rather than in structural barriers and patriarchal institutions governing ocean spaces.

### 3.6 Power Asymmetries and Political Economy

**Largely Unaddressed**: The Ocean Pact fails to critically examine power relations shaping ocean governance and use.

**Missing Political Economy Analysis**:
- No acknowledgment of corporate concentration in maritime industries (shipping, fishing, offshore energy)
- Absence of analysis regarding lobbying influence on ocean policy
- No discussion of how market power shapes access to marine resources and spaces
- Missing examination of North-South dynamics in global ocean governance
- No consideration of how financialization affects ocean governance (e.g., blue bonds, ocean-based natural capital)
- Absence of critical reflection on "blue growth" paradigm itself

**Implications**: By treating the ocean primarily as an economic opportunity space for "competitiveness" and "innovation," the pact naturalizes existing power structures rather than interrogating who has power to shape ocean futures and whose interests are privileged in ocean governance institutions.

### 3.7 Intergenerational Dimensions

**Partial Recognition**: The pact proposes "an intergenerational network of youth ambassadors for the ocean" and "annual dialogues with young people" plus "a new intergenerational blue program" for career development [9].

**Assessment**:
- Positive: explicit attention to intergenerational dialogue and youth engagement
- Limitation: youth participation appears consultative rather than involving genuine decision-making power
- Missing: broader intergenerational justice framework regarding resource depletion, climate impacts, and long-term sustainability
- Framing issue: youth engagement focused on career development (human capital) rather than rights or justice claims

## 4. Tensions Between Economic Growth and Social Sustainability Goals

The Ocean Pact embodies fundamental tensions between its economic growth imperatives and social/environmental sustainability commitments. These tensions reflect broader contradictions in the "sustainable development" paradigm and specifically in "blue growth" discourse.

### 4.1 The Blue Growth Paradigm

**Core Tension**: The pact aims to "boost the competitiveness of the blue economy" [1][4] while simultaneously ensuring sustainability. This reflects the "ecological modernization" assumption that economic growth and environmental protection are compatible through technological innovation and market-based mechanisms.

**Critical Questions**:
- Can continuous growth in ocean-based economic activities be reconciled with ocean health restoration?
- Does "sustainable" modify "growth" or does "growth" imperative override sustainability when conflicts arise?
- Who defines what counts as "sustainable" in blue economy development?

### 4.2 Specific Tensions Identified

**Maritime Spatial Competition**
The document acknowledges "increasing pressure on limited marine areas from various maritime activities, such as marine energy, fisheries, aquaculture, shipping, recreation, and coastal tourism" [16]. The proposed solution—"coordinated planning approach that balances individual and collective use"—assumes compatibility through better management rather than recognizing potential fundamental conflicts.

**Tension**: Expanding multiple ocean industries simultaneously (offshore renewables, aquaculture, shipping, tourism) while protecting 30% of marine areas and restoring ecosystems creates spatial and ecological contradictions. Who loses access when marine areas are allocated to industrial uses or conservation?

**Fisheries Modernization vs. Traditional Livelihoods**
The pact seeks to "increase competitiveness and resilience" of fisheries while "maintaining stable supplies to EU market" and "continuing to apply an ecosystem-based approach" [3]. It mentions "modernization of fishing fleets" [6].

**Tensions**:
- Fleet modernization often means larger, more efficient vessels that can displace small-scale fishers
- Competitiveness pressures favor industrial-scale operations over artisanal fisheries
- Market supply stability may prioritize efficiency over employment or community resilience
- Ecosystem-based management may restrict fishing in ways that differentially impact small-scale vs. industrial operators

**Resource Exploitation vs. Conservation**
The document highlights ocean as source of "genetic resources and raw materials, such as magnesium, lithium, boron, and vanadium from brines" [6] for economic benefit, while also stressing environmental protection.

**Tensions**:
- Marine genetic resources raise biopiracy concerns and benefit-sharing questions
- Mineral extraction from ocean (whether seabed mining or brine extraction) has uncertain ecological impacts
- Who benefits from commercializing marine resources vs. who bears environmental and social costs?
- Precautionary principle vs. innovation imperative

**Decarbonization vs. Economic Competitiveness**
The pact emphasizes "decarbonization of the maritime sector" [17][18] while maintaining competitiveness. It encourages "significant public and private investment" and leveraging "EU funds like InvestEU" [17][18].

**Tensions**:
- Decarbonization requires substantial investment that may disadvantage smaller operators
- Green technology transition may concentrate market power among actors with capital access
- Short-term competitiveness concerns may slow decarbonization pace
- Carbon pricing (ETS) impacts may be regressive, affecting small-scale operators more than large companies with economies of scale

### 4.3 Investment and Financialization Dynamics

**Capital Mobilization**: The pact emphasizes "significant public and private investment" [17][18] and highlights the "BlueInvest platform" for "blue economy start-ups and SMEs" [19].

**Social Sustainability Concerns**:
- Financialization of ocean governance may prioritize investor returns over social or environmental outcomes
- Public-private partnerships may transfer public resources to private profit
- Start-up and SME focus suggests entrepreneurial model rather than support for existing small-scale operators
- No mechanisms ensuring investment benefits coastal communities or addresses inequality

### 4.4 Security and Militarization

**Emerging Tension**: The pact's emphasis on "maritime security and defense" [5][10], including "naval and coast guard operations" and "advanced marine technologies," introduces securitization dynamics.

**Social Implications**:
- Securitization can restrict access to marine spaces for traditional users
- Military priorities may override environmental or social concerns
- Defense investments compete with social spending
- Geopolitical framing can marginalize sustainability concerns

### 4.5 The "Nature and People" Rhetoric

**Problematic Framing**: The pact repeatedly invokes business models that benefit "both nature and people" [5][6][7], suggesting inherent compatibility.

**Critical Perspective**:
- This "win-win" framing obscures real trade-offs and conflicts
- "People" is treated as undifferentiated category, masking distributional conflicts
- Nature is instrumentalized—valued for benefits to people rather than intrinsically
- Assumes market-based solutions can resolve social-ecological contradictions

## 5. Governance and Institutional Mechanisms: Social Science Critique

The Ocean Pact proposes various governance mechanisms that merit critical examination from social science perspectives, particularly regarding democratic legitimacy, power distribution, and capacity for addressing complex social-ecological challenges.

### 5.1 Proposed Governance Mechanisms

**Streamlined Governance Framework**
The pact proposes "a unified reference framework to improve coordination processes, simplify reporting obligations, and ensure more coherent implementation" [13], including an "Ocean Act" based on reviewing the Marine Spatial Planning Directive.

**High-Level Ocean Council**
A "high-level Ocean Council, comprising representatives from relevant ocean-related sectors" [14], modeled on the European Council for Agriculture and Food.

**Public Dashboard**
A "European Ocean Pact Dashboard" for monitoring "progress on indicators and objectives, using existing reports and digital tools like the EU Blue Economy Observatory and Copernicus" [15].

**Regional Cooperation**
Support for "regional cooperation through frameworks like regional fisheries management organizations and marine conventions" [16].

**Financial Mechanisms**
Leveraging "EU funds like InvestEU," encouraging Member States to allocate "revenues from the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) to decarbonize the maritime sector" [17][18], and utilizing the "BlueInvest platform" [19].

### 5.2 Social Science Critique: Democratic Legitimacy

**Technocratic Bias**
The governance architecture privileges technical expertise, sectoral representation, and administrative efficiency over democratic participation and deliberation.

**Limitations**:
- High-level councils of sectoral representatives may exclude or marginalize non-organized stakeholders
- Dashboard monitoring emphasizes quantifiable indicators, potentially missing qualitative social dimensions
- "Streamlining" and "simplification" may reduce opportunities for public input and contestation
- No mention of parliamentary oversight, citizen assemblies, or participatory budgeting

**Accountability Gaps**:
- Unclear how the Ocean Council will be accountable to affected communities
- No specification of transparency mechanisms or public access to decision-making processes
- Missing provisions for independent oversight or evaluation of social impacts

### 5.3 Scale and Subsidiarity Issues

**Top-Down Orientation**
The pact emphasizes EU-level coordination and frameworks, with limited attention to subsidiarity or local governance.

**Concerns**:
- Ocean governance challenges are place-based and context-specific, requiring local knowledge and adaptation
- EU-level frameworks may be insensitive to regional variations in social-ecological systems
- Centralized governance can disempower local communities and traditional management systems
- Regional cooperation mechanisms (mentioned but not detailed) may not adequately incorporate local stakeholders

**Missing Multi-Level Governance**:
- Insufficient articulation of how EU, national, regional, and local levels interact
- No discussion of how to resolve conflicts between governance scales
- Absence of mechanisms ensuring local communities can challenge or adapt EU-level policies

### 5.4 Sectoral vs. Integrated Governance

**Sectoral Representation Model**
The Ocean Council model, based on the European Council for Agriculture and Food, suggests sectoral representation.

**Limitations**:
- Sectoral approaches may perpetuate silos despite rhetoric of integration
- Organized sectors (industry associations) have more representation capacity than diffuse interests (citizens, future generations, ecosystems)
- Risk of regulatory capture by well-resourced sectoral actors
- Cross-cutting issues (justice, equity, cultural values) may fall between sectoral mandates

**Need for Truly Integrated Governance**:
Social science research on environmental governance emphasizes need for institutions that can:
- Address cross-scale interactions and feedbacks
- Incorporate diverse knowledge systems
- Enable adaptive management and learning
- Ensure procedural justice alongside environmental and distributional justice

The proposed mechanisms show limited capacity for such integration.

### 5.5 Knowledge Politics and Epistemic Authority

**Scientific Privileging**
The pact emphasizes "science-based decision-making" and "ocean observation initiative" [5][8][9], positioning natural science and technology as primary knowledge sources.

**Social Science Critique**:
- Narrow scientism excludes other knowledge forms (traditional, experiential, local)
- Science is not neutral; research agendas reflect funding, institutional priorities, and power relations
- Technical knowledge alone cannot resolve normative questions about values, justice, and priorities
- Missing recognition that ocean governance involves "post-normal" problems characterized by uncertainty, value conflicts, and high stakes

**Alternative Approaches**:
- Transdisciplinary knowledge co-production involving scientists, practitioners, and communities
- Deliberative processes for negotiating knowledge claims and values
- Recognition of "extended peer communities" in quality assurance of knowledge for policy
- Integration of social science and humanities alongside natural science

### 5.6 Monitoring and Indicators

**Dashboard Approach**
The "European Ocean Pact Dashboard" [15] using "existing reports and digital tools" suggests quantitative indicator-based monitoring.

**Limitations from Social Science Perspective**:
- Indicators privilege measurable over meaningful: What counts gets counted, what gets counted counts
- Quantitative metrics may miss qualitative dimensions of social sustainability (community cohesion, cultural values, subjective well-being)
- Aggregated indicators can mask distributional inequalities and local variations
- Dashboard metaphor suggests technical management rather than democratic accountability
- Risk of "governing by indicators" where improving metrics becomes goal rather than addressing underlying problems

**Missing Social Indicators**:
The document doesn't specify social indicators for monitoring. Comprehensive social sustainability monitoring would require:
- Income distribution and poverty rates in coastal communities
- Employment quality (not just quantity) in maritime sectors
- Gender equity metrics across ocean-related activities
- Community participation and satisfaction with governance processes
- Cultural vitality and maintenance of maritime heritage
- Access to marine resources across social groups
- Health and well-being of ocean-dependent communities

### 5.7 Financial Governance and Conditionality

**Investment-Centric Approach**
Heavy emphasis on mobilizing public and private investment [17][18][19] raises governance questions.

**Concerns**:
- Public funding leverage for private investment may prioritize investor interests
- Conditionality attached to EU funds can constrain Member State and local autonomy
- Financial mechanisms may favor large-scale projects over small-scale initiatives
- Unclear how social and environmental standards will be enforced for funded activities
- Risk of "blue washing"—projects marketed as sustainable without substantive standards

**Alternative Approaches**:
- Community-controlled funds with participatory allocation
- Social economy and cooperative models alongside private investment
- Strict social and environmental conditionality with independent monitoring
- Public ownership options for critical ocean infrastructure

### 5.8 Enforcement and Compliance

**Weak Enforcement Provisions**
The pact emphasizes coordination and cooperation but provides limited detail on enforcement.

**Gaps**:
- No specification of sanctions for non-compliance with sustainability commitments
- Unclear how conflicts between Member States or sectors will be resolved
- Missing mechanisms for citizens or communities to hold actors accountable
- Voluntary approaches may be insufficient for addressing powerful economic interests

**Comparison to Other Domains**:
EU environmental policy has been most effective when combining clear standards, monitoring, enforcement, and access to justice (e.g., environmental impact assessment, habitats directives). The Ocean Pact's governance mechanisms appear weaker in these dimensions.

### 5.9 Adaptive Governance and Learning

**Limited Adaptive Capacity**
The pact emphasizes coordination and monitoring but less attention to learning and adaptation.

**Social-Ecological Systems Perspective**:
Research on governing complex social-ecological systems emphasizes:
- Adaptive management: learning from implementation and adjusting strategies
- Polycentricity: multiple decision-making centers enabling experimentation and redundancy
- Bridging organizations: connecting across scales and sectors
- Social learning: collective processes of developing shared understanding

The proposed governance mechanisms show limited capacity for such adaptive governance, instead suggesting more static coordination and monitoring.

### 5.10 International Dimensions and Power Asymmetries

**EU External Relations**
The pact emphasizes EU ocean diplomacy and international governance [5][11][12], including upholding UNCLOS, ratifying BBNJ, and combating IUU fishing.

**Critical Questions**:
- How does EU ocean policy affect developing countries dependent on fisheries and marine resources?
- Do EU sustainability standards imposed on trading partners constitute "green imperialism" or necessary environmental protection?
- How are power asymmetries between EU and partner countries addressed in fisheries agreements and marine cooperation?
- Does EU "leadership" in ocean governance reflect genuine multilateralism or EU interest projection?

**Missing Analysis**:
- No discussion of how EU ocean policies affect global equity
- Absent consideration of historical responsibility for ocean degradation
- Limited attention to capacity building and technology transfer to developing countries
- No mention of climate justice dimensions of ocean governance

## 6. Synthesis and Theoretical Implications

### 6.1 Dominant Discourse: Blue Growth and Ecological Modernization

The Ocean Pact is fundamentally structured by "blue growth" discourse—the marine equivalent of "green growth"—assuming that economic expansion and environmental sustainability are compatible through innovation, efficiency, and market-based mechanisms.

**Theoretical Framework**:
This reflects "ecological modernization" theory, which posits that environmental problems can be solved within existing capitalist economic structures through technological innovation, regulatory reform, and market instruments.

**Critical Perspective**:
Political ecology and ecological economics scholarship questions whether:
- Continuous growth is compatible with planetary boundaries
- Market mechanisms adequately address environmental and social externalities
- Technological optimism is justified given rebound effects and systemic risks
- Current power structures can deliver equitable sustainability transitions

The Ocean Pact's tensions between growth and sustainability reflect these broader theoretical debates.

### 6.2 Absent Paradigms: Rights, Commons, Degrowth

**Rights-Based Approaches**
Conspicuously absent from the Ocean Pact is rights-based framing:
- Rights of nature or ocean ecosystems
- Human rights to healthy ocean environment
- Rights of coastal and fishing communities to livelihoods and culture
- Rights of future generations to ocean resources

**Commons Governance**
Despite rich scholarship on governing common-pool resources (Ostrom and successors), the pact shows limited engagement with commons principles:
- Community-based management and co-governance
- Polycentric governance systems
- Traditional and customary tenure systems
- Collective action and cooperation beyond market and state

**Degrowth and Post-Growth**
The pact doesn't engage with emerging debates about:
- Degrowth: planned reduction of resource throughput in wealthy regions
- Post-growth: economies designed for well-being rather than GDP growth
- Sufficiency: meeting needs through less consumption rather than more efficient production

These paradigms offer alternative framings for ocean sustainability but remain marginalized in EU policy discourse.

### 6.3 Social Dimensions as Afterthought

**Pattern of Superficial Integration**
Across dimensions—labor, participation, justice, traditional knowledge, gender—the Ocean Pact shows a pattern:
1. Brief rhetorical acknowledgment
2. Absence of substantive analysis or integration
3. No specific mechanisms, standards, or accountability
4. Framing as secondary to economic and environmental priorities

**Implications**:
- Social sustainability is treated as "add-on" rather than core dimension
- Economic and environmental goals are privileged; social considerations are constraints or co-benefits
- Lack of social science integration in policy development
- Missed opportunities for social innovation in ocean governance

### 6.4 Governance as Technocratic Coordination

**Dominant Governance Rationality**
The pact embodies technocratic rationality:
- Problems are technical, requiring expert knowledge and coordination
- Solutions involve better data, streamlined procedures, and efficient management
- Legitimacy derives from expertise and effectiveness rather than democratic participation
- Politics and power are rendered invisible through technical framing

**Democratic Deficit**:
From democratic theory perspective, this raises concerns:
- Limited space for deliberation about values and priorities
- Exclusion of non-expert voices and knowledge
- Accountability to sectoral interests rather than citizens
- Depoliticization of inherently political choices about ocean futures

### 6.5 Spatial Justice and the Politics of Marine Space

**Marine Spatial Planning as Contested**
The pact treats marine spatial planning primarily as technical coordination challenge rather than political process involving:
- Competing claims to ocean space
- Unequal power to influence spatial allocations
- Distributional consequences of spatial decisions
- Cultural and symbolic meanings of marine places

**Need for Spatial Justice Framework**:
Critical geography and spatial justice scholarship emphasizes:
- Space is socially produced through power relations
- Spatial arrangements reflect and reproduce inequalities
- Just sustainability requires attention to spatial equity
- Participation must include power to shape spatial outcomes

### 6.6 Missing: Transformative Change

**Incremental vs. Transformative**
The Ocean Pact proposes incremental improvements—better coordination, more investment, cleaner technologies—within existing structures.

**Transformation Scholarship**:
Sustainability science increasingly emphasizes need for transformative change:
- Fundamental shifts in values, power relations, and institutions
- Challenging dominant paradigms and vested interests
- Deep democracy and empowerment of marginalized actors
- Radical innovation in governance, economy, and society

The Ocean Pact shows limited engagement with transformative change imperatives, instead assuming existing governance and economic structures can deliver sustainability through optimization.

## 7. Conclusions and Research Implications

### 7.1 Key Findings

**1. Comprehensive Framework with Narrow Framing**
The Ocean Pact provides comprehensive policy framework across ocean issues but within narrow blue growth paradigm that privileges economic competitiveness and technocratic governance.

**2. Marginalization of Social Dimensions**
Social dimensions—labor rights, community participation, distributive justice, traditional knowledge, gender equity—receive superficial acknowledgment without substantive integration or accountability mechanisms.

**3. Unresolved Tensions**
Fundamental tensions between economic growth imperatives and social/environmental sustainability remain unresolved, papered over with "win-win" rhetoric that obscures real trade-offs and conflicts.

**4. Democratic Deficits**
Proposed governance mechanisms show limited capacity for democratic participation, accountability, and addressing power asymmetries, favoring technocratic coordination and sectoral representation.

**5. Epistemic Narrowness**
Heavy reliance on natural science and technology, with limited integration of social science, humanities, traditional knowledge, or experiential learning from coastal communities.

### 7.2 Critical Gaps Requiring Further Research

Given the inaccessibility of the Seas At Risk critique for this analysis, priority research questions include:

**Environmental NGO Critiques**:
- What specific environmental protection gaps does Seas At Risk identify?
- What concerns about greenwashing or weak enforcement are raised?
- How do civil society actors assess the adequacy of biodiversity and climate provisions?
- What alternative policy frameworks do environmental advocates propose?

**Social Movement Perspectives**:
- How do fishing communities, coastal residents, and maritime workers assess the Ocean Pact?
- What are grassroots alternatives to top-down blue growth strategies?
- How do social movements frame ocean justice?

**Comparative Governance Analysis**:
- How does EU Ocean Pact compare to ocean governance in other regions (e.g., Pacific Island nations with more explicit rights-based and indigenous approaches)?
- What can be learned from more participatory and community-based ocean governance experiments?

**Implementation and Outcomes**:
- How is the Ocean Pact being implemented in practice?
- What are actual distributional outcomes across social groups and regions?
- Are governance mechanisms enabling meaningful participation or reproducing exclusions?

### 7.3 Recommendations for Policy and Research

**For Policy Development**:
1. **Integrate Social Science**: Ensure social scientists, including anthropologists, sociologists, geographers, and political scientists, are involved in ocean policy development alongside natural scientists
2. **Rights-Based Frameworks**: Develop explicit rights-based approaches addressing community rights, labor rights, and rights of nature
3. **Substantive Justice Mechanisms**: Move beyond rhetoric to concrete mechanisms ensuring distributive, procedural, and recognition justice
4. **Democratic Governance**: Create spaces for genuine public deliberation and community participation in decision-making, not just consultation
5. **Traditional Knowledge Integration**: Establish frameworks for respectfully incorporating traditional and local knowledge alongside scientific knowledge
6. **Gender Mainstreaming**: Conduct gender analysis and implement gender mainstreaming across all ocean policies
7. **Power Analysis**: Explicitly address power asymmetries and develop mechanisms for empowering marginalized actors

**For Research Agendas**:
1. **Political Economy of Blue Growth**: Critical analysis of who benefits and who bears costs of blue economy expansion
2. **Ocean Justice Scholarship**: Develop theoretical and empirical work on justice dimensions of ocean governance
3. **Participatory Governance Experiments**: Study and support experiments in community-based and co-governance approaches
4. **Social Impacts Assessment**: Develop methodologies for assessing social impacts of ocean policies
5. **Intersectional Analysis**: Examine how gender, class, ethnicity, and geography intersect in shaping ocean-related opportunities and vulnerabilities
6. **Alternative Paradigms**: Explore commons governance, degrowth, and rights-based alternatives to blue growth
7. **Comparative Studies**: Learn from diverse governance approaches globally, particularly from regions with strong indigenous and community governance traditions

### 7.4 Final Reflection

The European Ocean Pact represents an ambitious attempt to coordinate ocean governance across multiple sectors and scales. However, this analysis reveals significant limitations in how social dimensions are framed and integrated. The dominance of blue growth discourse, technocratic governance approaches, and economic competitiveness imperatives leaves limited space for addressing fundamental questions of justice, democracy, and power in ocean governance.

A truly sustainable and just ocean future requires moving beyond technical optimization within existing structures toward transformative change that centers social equity, democratic participation, and diverse knowledge systems. This demands not just better coordination but deeper engagement with political economy, power relations, and alternative paradigms for human-ocean relationships.

The absence of the Seas At Risk critique from this analysis represents a significant limitation, as civil society perspectives often identify critical gaps invisible in official documents. Future research must incorporate these critical voices alongside academic analysis to develop more complete understanding of contested terrain in EU ocean governance.
