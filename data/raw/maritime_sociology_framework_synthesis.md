## TL;DR

Blue Sociology builds on maritime sociology’s long empirical and theoretical work on labour, organization, and territorial change while incorporating oceanic-turn insights (more-than-human, hydrosocial, materialist) to address justice, governance, and labour tensions across Blue Economy sectors. It fills conceptual and methodological gaps by foregrounding socioecological relations and multi-scalar power analysis.

## Theoretical foundations

This section connects established maritime sociology insights to analytic entry points for a Blue Sociology framework in the Blue Economy. It highlights core empirical domains and theoretical traditions that already provide a firm base for extension.

Maritime sociology contributes sustained empirical knowledge on seafarers, shipboard organization, port communities, and labour regimes, showing how globalized shipping practices produce precarious labour, segmented skill regimes, and regulated vulnerabilities [1] [2] [3] [4].  
Key theoretical traditions and contributions include the following:
- **Political economy of maritime industries** Evidence on profit-driven restructuring, flags of convenience, and weak enforcement under globalization foregrounds power and regulation as central analytic nodes [2] [3].  
- **Organizational sociology of shipboard life** Studies of crew social structures and shipboard organizational forms supply methods for analysing social integration, job satisfaction, and skill-use in mobile workplaces [1].  
- **Labour process and skill regimes** Research documenting automation, standardisation of qualifications, and racialised skill hierarchies links technologized work to evolving inequalities onboard and ashore [5] [6].  
- **Territorial and sociotechnical trajectory approaches** Analyses of port-driven territorial change demonstrate how maritimization shapes urban and regional authority and legitimacy over time [6].  
- **Socioecological and environmental sociology links** Calls to integrate ecological foundations and social metabolism into marine studies position maritime sociology for socioecological analysis of ocean use [7].

Each of these traditions provides analytic tools—power/actor mapping, labour-process analysis, institutional history, and socioecological framing—that can be redeployed to examine Blue Economy governance, justice, and transformation [1] [7].

## Oceanic turn perspectives

This section maps how contemporary scholarship reframes oceans and sea–society relations and the key theoretical innovations relevant to Blue Sociology. It synthesizes more-than-human, hydrosocial, and materialist orientations.

Scholars of the oceanic turn reconceptualize seas as relational, political, and lived spaces rather than inert resources, foregrounding multispecies, material, and affective entanglements between humans and marine environments [8] [9].  
Principal theoretical innovations include:
- **More‑than‑human geographies** Analyses that attend to nonhuman actors and ecologies show how fisheries, reefs, and species co-produce social practices and governance problems [8].  
- **Hydrosocial perspectives** Work reframing water as a social and political medium emphasizes flows, infrastructures, and power in shaping human–ocean relations and territorial claims [9] [10].  
- **Materialist ontologies and socioecological metabolism** Approaches that combine social metabolic analysis with maritime studies link industrial extraction, pollution, and climate impacts to social restructuring and inequality [7] [11].  
- **Oceanic imaginaries and cultural critique** Literary and historical oceanic criticism reveals how ocean narratives shape identities, migration experiences, and policy imaginaries for oceanic development [9].

These perspectives push analysis beyond sectoral economics to attend to embodied practices, infrastructural assemblages, and nonhuman agency—methodological moves essential for an integrated Blue Sociology [8] [7].

## Blue economy tensions and gaps

This section synthesizes recurring tensions reported across EU Blue Economy sectors and identifies conceptual and methodological gaps that a Blue Sociology can address. It links empirical tensions to governance and justice questions.

EU-level analyses and sector studies reveal recurring tensions over distributive justice, participation, labour regimes, and social sustainability as Blue Economy initiatives expand economic activities in marine spaces [12].  
Observed sectoral tensions and governance gaps include:
- **Distributive justice and access** Conflicts over who benefits from resource development, and historical lessons indicate equitable access and responsive management are crucial for durable blue growth [13] [12].  
- **Participation and legitimacy** Co-management and local governance instruments (e.g., FLAGs, MSP) often lack capacity or inclusive practices to secure equitable outcomes and community buy‑in [14] [11].  
- **Labour regimes and precarity** Shipping and associated sectors show persistent labour vulnerabilities, weak collective power, and regulatory enforcement gaps tied to profit-maximising structures [2] [3] [5].  
- **Social sustainability and localisation** Emerging industries (marine renewables, aquaculture, tourism) create local disruptions—social impacts often precede perceived benefits, requiring early and sustained social engagement [15] [16].  

Conceptual and methodological gaps that maritime sociology can help fill include:
- **Multi-scalar socioecological integration** Existing Blue Economy frameworks insufficiently integrate ecological dynamics with social processes across scales; maritime sociology’s socioecological and historical methods can bridge this gap [11] [7].  
- **Labour‑centred social analysis across sectors** Labour and work relations are under‑analysed in many Blue Economy policy discussions despite shaping distributional outcomes; maritime labour studies offer robust tools for this analysis [2] [5].  
- **Inclusion of more‑than‑human and material infrastructures** Policy framings often omit nonhuman agency and infrastructural politics; oceanic-turn methods reintroduce these as analytical variables [8] [10].  
- **Democratic and place‑based mechanisms** Studies show a paucity of effective urban–sea participatory mechanisms (blue democracy) that link coastal communities to marine governance [16] [17].

Addressing these gaps requires cross-disciplinary methods—ethnography, institutional history, political economy, and socioecological modelling—grounded in maritime empirical domains [11] [7].

## Analytical renewal and integration

This section explains how Blue Sociology can act as a complementary extension of maritime sociology by integrating oceanic-turn insights, governance studies, circularity, and more-than-human approaches. It proposes programmatic directions for research and practice.

Blue Sociology should synthesise maritime sociology’s empirical strengths with oceanic-turn theoretical moves to produce analyses that are both labour- and socioecology-centred and attuned to multispecies and infrastructural politics [1] [8] [7]. Recommended integrative emphases are:
- **Labour‑centred socioecological inquiry** Prioritise studies of labour regimes across Blue Economy sectors to trace how work, safety, and social reproduction mediate socioecological outcomes [2] [5].  
- **Hydrosocial and infrastructural analysis** Combine MSP and land–sea interaction frameworks with hydrosocial approaches to understand how flows, infrastructures, and jurisdictions shape distributive outcomes [11] [10].  
- **Democratic place‑based governance** Develop research on urban MPAs, blue democracy, and participatory planning to strengthen legitimacy and local stewardship in coastal cities [16] [12].  
- **Historical and comparative lessons** Use longue durée and comparative cases (historic blue‑growth successes/failures) to inform equitable Blue Economy policy design [13].  
- **Methodological pluralism** Integrate ethnography, institutional history, political economy, and socioecological metrics to capture multi‑scalar dynamics and nonhuman agencies [7] [8].

By positioning itself as complementary, Blue Sociology can supply the social diagnostics, theory-building, and methodological toolkits needed to make Blue Economy transitions socially just, ecologically attuned, and democratically legitimate [11] [7].
