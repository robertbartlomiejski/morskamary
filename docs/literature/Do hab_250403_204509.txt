By the technological revolution, the port-city concept, their relations, and their interface have been well developed and examined in the literature, using various qualitative and quantitative dimensions (see Table 1). In general, by the early history of shipping and freight distribution, ports and cities were functionally and geographically intertwined (Hayuth, 1982). However, with the advancement of transportation technology, port-city relations have been transformed. Port-cities, as linking nodes, are powerful manifestations of global flows, and international integration, and can be recognized as the ancestors of global cities and examples of globalization (Tan, 2007). The port and the city can be compared to a nested system in which the port is one part of the city. The port and the city do not have a direct relationship with the creation of multimodal businesses. in addition, after globalization and the container revolution, the role of the port as an economic driver for the city has decreased (Chen & Lam, 2018). In other words, ports are no longer just a functional node for the transportation of goods, but also, consider a scene in which cultures, historical identities, policies, economies, organizations and institutions, knowledge, employment, and societies meet and interact (Tan, 2007).

The city and the port, in the interface, collide and affect each other’s development or decline by creating complex relationships. As a concept, the portcity interface was introduced in the 1980s. This was based on the theory that port and urban economies are increasingly disconnected (Hayuth, 1982; Hoyle, 1989). At that time, the concept was introduced based on earlier concepts within the geographical literature on the evolution of ports, especially James Bird (1963) (Berghe et al., 2018). At any given moment, there are more than 50,000 ships crossing the ocean or loading or unloading at ports around the world. These ships transport 90% of all global trade, and in the United States, 95% of cargo arrives by ship. That means if you look around you, most of what you see probably traveled at some point on a ship. Much of modern life as we know it would not be possible without the maritime shipping industry, but alongside its benefits come devastating impacts.

https://www.ics-shipping.org/shipping-fact/shipping-and-world-trade-world-seaborne-trade/

Shipping is the life blood of the global economy. Without shipping, intercontinental trade, the bulk transport of raw materials, and the import/export of affordable food and manufactured goods would simply not be possible.

Seaborne trade continues to expand, bringing benefits for consumers across the world through competitive freight costs. Thanks to the growing efficiency of shipping as a mode of transport and increased economic liberalisation, the prospects for the industry’s further growth continue to be strong.

There are over 50,000 merchant ships trading internationally, transporting every kind of cargo. The world fleet is registered in over 150 nations, and manned by over a million seafarers of virtually every nationality.

Ships are technically sophisticated, high value assets (larger hi-tech vessels can cost over US $200 million to build), and the operation of merchant ships generates an estimated annual income of over half a trillion US Dollars in freight rates.

World Seaborne Trade

It is difficult to quantify the value of volume of world seaborne trade in monetary terms, as figures for trade estimates are traditionally in terms of tonnes or tonne-miles, and are therefore not comparable with monetary-based statistics for the value of the world economy.

However, the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD) estimates that the operation of merchant ships contributes about US$380 billion in freight rates within the global economy, equivalent to about 5% of total world trade.

Shipping trade estimates are often calculated in tonne-miles, as a way of measuring the volume of trade (or “transportation work “, as it is sometimes referred).

Advances in technology have also made shipping an increasingly efficient and swift method of transportation. Over the last four decades total seaborne trade estimates have quadrupled, from just over 8 thousand billion tonne-miles in 1968 to over 32 thousand billion tonne-miles in 2008.

Introduction to October’s theme

Milena Kremakova

8 października 2024

Welcome aboard the October issue of The Sociological Review magazine. In our ship’s cargo hold, we have stowed a sociological treasure trove that includes tales of refugee migrants, fishers, tugboats, shipworms and underwater cables; you’ll also encounter Godzilla (a hydrothermal vent), maritime museums and sociological sailing ships.

The sea has always been a point where human civilisation meets nature. Sometimes this happens peacefully, but there’s often a clash. The sea is a powerful trope that continues to fuel creativity, innovation and dreams for a better future. However, it also represents a chthonic site of invisible – or historically ignored – socioeconomic injustices. It is central to the biodiversity and climate of our planet, yet is often plundered. It is a barrier, but also a bridge between, cultures and communities. It is a rich source of sustenance, wealth, narratives and political power but also a place of rough natural elements and a fragile ecosystem. It is a daily reminder that being human also requires humility and responsibility. Yet – oddly enough – sociology has only recently begun to notice the blue elephant in the room.

The sea fuels creativity, innovation and dreams for a better future. But it also represents a chthonic site of invisible socioeconomic injustices.

These days, discussions on the topic of the sea often reference climate change, specifically how it intersects with the loss of traditional livelihoods as a result of the pressures of global capitalism. Jennifer Telesca examines how people in suits make decisions about the planetary ecosystem over a multi-course lunch. She argues that nature is more than just a resource and that it should not be for sale. Yet, we humans continue to trade it for short-term profit to the detriment of the planet.

This is an argument we urgently need to hear frequently and loudly. Telesca’s research into Norway’s blue economy and the political and technological realities of deep-sea mining makes a strong case that the real danger in the room is our arrogance and irresponsibility in dealing with nature.

Cláudia Santos, Mashood Djaló and José Júnior Mendes transport us to the island of Soga, one of the 88 islands that form the Bijagós Archipelago in Guinea-Bissau, West Africa. They highlight the ongoing dramatic changes in sea fishing practices, which endanger biodiversity and disrupt livelihoods. Their account unpacks the complexities of balancing the unstoppable march of global capitalism with the scarcity of fishing resources, traditional fishing cultures and local people’s lives.

Some 5,000 km to the north, on the Mediterranean island of Mallorca, mass tourism has a similar effect on the environment and local communities. Daniel Albero Santacreu exposes the harmful effects of this “touristification” in his article and short film. He argues that maritime museums often fixate on upholding a heroic image of the past, and that, as a result, they miss a valuable opportunity to address the challenges maritime communities face today. He proposes that by imagining tomorrow’s museum, heritage institutions can play a strategic role in helping us reimagine our relationship with the sea and create a better future for maritime communities.

People have always employed technologies to traverse the vast sea and connect distant lands, both on and under the water. Have you ever wondered what giant cables and tiny worms have in common? For one thing, they meet in the ocean. “Global worming”, a phrase that my autocorrect kept trying to amend, is more than a cheeky play on words.

Historian Amedeo Policante tells us a sociohistorical detective story by compellingly linking these two unlikely non-human protagonists in the history of deep-sea industrialisation, demonstrating how they simultaneously enable and jeopardise the pivotal technologies essential for sustaining daily life worldwide. Ships move the world’s goods and cables make the internet possible. But the worms eat the ships and the cables in an epic arms race with exasperated shipowners and governments.

Take a deep breath and slow down (I want to say “close your eyes”) while reading Aurora Ribeiro’s almost cinematic article. Unwind as she invites you to dive through layers of socionatural complexity and immerses you in the ocean. Ribeiro argues that the new technosciences are changing the relationship of the Azores and Azoreans with their sea, opening up new possibilities, while also presenting new dangers.

Two contributions share insights about life on modern-day sailing vessels and show how sociological research is not necessarily a land-based activity. While the authors take us beyond the typical romanticised portrayal of seafaring life, it’s clear that they have a deep emotional connection to the sea, which is reflected in their writing. Christiaan de Beukelaer reports on a three-day academic workshop, called One Planet Shipping, that took place in the open seas. He argues that physically being on a ship and experiencing its physical and social constraints can help us achieve a deeper appreciation of our planet’s finite resources.

By contrast, Christine Handte has become a “sea native”, having lived and worked on the RV Heraclitus since 1986. She is the expedition chief of this unique sailboat. I was lucky to catch her and Sofia Alexiou, bosun’s mate and research assistant, on shore for a conversation about their ship’s sociological adventures. The Heraclitus, a versatile research vessel, is engaged in fascinating studies, including a sociological exploration of the effects of rapid ongoing climate change on coastal communities all over the Mediterranean and Atlantic coasts.

The sea is also a site of deeply anchored historical injustices. Daniel Tödt urges us to wonder why so many footballers on European teams are Black and who picks the strawberries that we expect to find in the supermarket all year round. He contends that social historians have unjustly ignored the “Black Mediterranean” and that the social history of Africa and the Mediterranean has yet to be written. To do this would require the Global North to confront the heritage of oppression, something that continues to this day in the illegalisation of migration from Africa.

The maritime industries are pivotal for our history, economies, cultures and ways of life and sociologists can be so much more than sea-blind landlubbers.

The theme of this year’s World Maritime Day, which took place on 26 September, was Navigating the future: safety first! – reflecting the International Maritime Organization’s work to improve maritime safety and security while protecting the marine environment. Benjamin Anderson sheds light on the difficult working conditions of tugboat workers in ports in Canada and draws our attention to the local impacts of the global shipping system. Towing barges, docking ships, guiding vessels and other small boats used to be a constant presence in coastal cities and towns, along with their large cousins, the cargo ships. But we now live in an era of mega ports, mega ships and mega cities. Despite tugboats and their crews still being crucial for the smooth, speedy and safe movement of commodities around the globe, they are increasingly hidden and are no longer visible to the public in the local landscape of redeveloped city centres.

In this issue’s illustration, Sierra Leonean-American artist Abi Mustapha reminds us of how the sea encapsulates a terrain of transportation and navigation. From humans and cargo to internet cable data and sea animals and plants, our oceans accommodate vast travelling societies that often go unnoticed.

When author and journalist Rose George talked about “sea blindness”, she referred to the puzzling observation that most of the Earth’s population knows and cares little about ships and people working on them beyond the romantic stereotypes. As a saltwater native, I did not believe this until I encountered sea blindness first hand during my doctoral research on the dramatic transformation of maritime industries and livelihoods on the Black Sea coast after the fall of the Iron Curtain. The maritime industries are pivotal for our history, economies, cultures and ways of life and sociologists can be so much more than sea-blind landlubbers.

I am writing this introduction on a balcony facing the bay of Bourgas in Bulgaria, and seeing the familiar story of my seaside town echoed again and again in the maritime tales of this issue, whether in Canada, Guinea-Bissau, Mallorca or the Azores. As Christina Handte says, thanks to the sea “things that feel local are often intensely interrelated – within the natural environment and also in human cultures”. I hope that exploring this issue of the magazine expands your perspective and enables you to imagine new possibilities beyond the horizon.

Milena Kremakova

Milena Kremakova is a sociologist and ethnographer, and a researcher on a labour studies project involving scholars at Pingtung University, Taiwan and Royal Holloway, University of London. Her areas of research include mathematics as work and social endeavour, precarious academic careers, the labour of knowledge reproduction, and the impact of marketisation and digitalisation on work. She previously focused on maritime labour beyond the Iron Curtain. She is part of the supervisory team for the University of Manchester MA in Digital Technologies, Communication and Education. For one thing, they meet in the ocean. “Global worming”, a phrase that my autocorrect kept trying to amend, is more than a cheeky play on words.

Historian Amedeo Policante tells us a sociohistorical detective story by compellingly linking these two unlikely non-human protagonists in the history of deep-sea industrialisation, demonstrating how they simultaneously enable and jeopardise the pivotal technologies essential for sustaining daily life worldwide. Ships move the world’s goods and cables make the internet possible. But the worms eat the ships and the cables in an epic arms race with exasperated shipowners and governments.

Take a deep breath and slow down (I want to say “close your eyes”) while reading Aurora Ribeiro’s almost cinematic article. Unwind as she invites you to dive through layers of socionatural complexity and immerses you in the ocean. Ribeiro argues that the new technosciences are changing the relationship of the Azores and Azoreans with their sea, opening up new possibilities, while also presenting new dangers.

Two contributions share insights about life on modern-day sailing vessels and show how sociological research is not necessarily a land-based activity. While the authors take us beyond the typical romanticised portrayal of seafaring life, it’s clear that they have a deep emotional connection to the sea, which is reflected in their writing. Christiaan de Beukelaer reports on a three-day academic workshop, called One Planet Shipping, that took place in the open seas. He argues that physically being on a ship and experiencing its physical and social constraints can help us achieve a deeper appreciation of our planet’s finite resources.

By contrast, Christine Handte has become a “sea native”, having lived and worked on the RV Heraclitus since 1986. She is the expedition chief of this unique sailboat. I was lucky to catch her and Sofia Alexiou, bosun’s mate and research assistant, on shore for a conversation about their ship’s sociological adventures. The Heraclitus, a versatile research vessel, is engaged in fascinating studies, including a sociological exploration of the effects of rapid ongoing climate change on coastal communities all over the Mediterranean and Atlantic coasts.

The sea is also a site of deeply anchored historical injustices. Daniel Tödt urges us to wonder why so many footballers on European teams are Black and who picks the strawberries that we expect to find in the supermarket all year round. He contends that social historians have unjustly ignored the “Black Mediterranean” and that the social history of Africa and the Mediterranean has yet to be written. To do this would require the Global North to confront the heritage of oppression, something that continues to this day in the illegalisation of migration from Africa.

The maritime industries are pivotal for our history, economies, cultures and ways of life and sociologists can be so much more than sea-blind landlubbers.

The theme of this year’s World Maritime Day, which took place on 26 September, was Navigating the future: safety first! – reflecting the International Maritime Organization’s work to improve maritime safety and security while protecting the marine environment. Benjamin Anderson sheds light on the difficult working conditions of tugboat workers in ports in Canada and draws our attention to the local impacts of the global shipping system. Towing barges, docking ships, guiding vessels and other small boats used to be a constant presence in coastal cities and towns, along with their large cousins, the cargo ships. But we now live in an era of mega ports, mega ships and mega cities. Despite tugboats and their crews still being crucial for the smooth, speedy and safe movement of commodities around the globe, they are increasingly hidden and are no longer visible to the public in the local landscape of redeveloped city centres.

In this issue’s illustration, Sierra Leonean-American artist Abi Mustapha reminds us of how the sea encapsulates a terrain of transportation and navigation. From humans and cargo to internet cable data and sea animals and plants, our oceans accommodate vast travelling societies that often go unnoticed.

When author and journalist Rose George talked about “sea blindness”, she referred to the puzzling observation that most of the Earth’s population knows and cares little about ships and people working on them beyond the romantic stereotypes. As a saltwater native, I did not believe this until I encountered sea blindness first hand during my doctoral research on the dramatic transformation of maritime industries and livelihoods on the Black Sea coast after the fall of the Iron Curtain. The maritime industries are pivotal for our history, economies, cultures and ways of life and sociologists can be so much more than sea-blind landlubbers.

I am writing this introduction on a balcony facing the bay of Bourgas in Bulgaria, and seeing the familiar story of my seaside town echoed again and again in the maritime tales of this issue, whether in Canada, Guinea-Bissau, Mallorca or the Azores. As Christina Handte says, thanks to the sea “things that feel local are often intensely interrelated – within the natural environment and also in human cultures”. I hope that exploring this issue of the magazine expands your perspective and enables you to imagine new possibilities beyond the horizon.

Boring storiesWhat connects 18th-century shipping to today’s global media network?

Amedeo Policante

8 października 2024

The humble shipworm meets a giant network of cables underneath the sea. We explore the interspecies conflict that’s been thwarting human ambitions to dominate the ocean for centuries.

Ever-expanding chains of industrial production and logistical distribution span the global ocean. Cargo ships unceasingly crisscross its surface, transporting over 90 per cent of internationally traded commodities. Meanwhile, the ocean floor has become a global infrastructural space, where thousands of kilometres of submarine cables enable the instantaneous flow of data, feeding the world wide web.

These infrastructures, which facilitate the global economy, do not operate in isolation. They interact with marine ecologies in multifarious and unforeseeable ways. They perturb complex ecosystem dynamics while being profoundly shaped by those very perturbations.

This is not the first face-off between marine biology and industry. Since the eighteenth century, shipworms ­– a folk name for all sorts of marine critters feeding on wood – ­have spread worldwide, drilling their way through colonial fleets, merchant vessels, wooden piers and cable webs. By metabolising and hollowing out global infrastructure networks, they have forcefully reshaped technologies of logistics and communication. As engineers of negative space, they have created the world by subtraction.

A history of destruction

When did this incessant gnawing begin? No one knows for certain, but the issue has been the subject of heated debates since the winter of 1730, when dikes and harbours along the Dutch coast started to crumble under their own weight. Inspections revealed an unprecedented shipworm infestation. “Who has seen a stranger plague?” questioned the anonymous author of a 1732 pamphlet discussing the appearance of this “rare pest, from God’s vengeful hand”.

An 18th-century Dutch engraving depicting the great shipworm epidemic.

Abraham Zeeman (1731)

It was the beginning of the so-called “great shipworm epidemic”. Worms proliferated within ships and ocean infrastructures across Northern Europe, tunnelling their way into the Western imagination. This new challenge prompted European naturalists to turn their attention towards the sea. Treatises discussing shipworms’ anatomy, biological cycles and ecological interactions were quickly produced and distributed. This soon led biologists to formally classify the naval shipworm (teredo navalis), ­a species of mollusc with the capacity to tunnel through, digest, and rapidly decompose submerged wood.

The causes of the shipworm epidemics, however, remained unclear. Some natural historians argued that colonial vessels returning from the East Indies had brought back to Europe not only wealth plundered in faraway lands, but also tropical varieties of shipworms hidden in their hulls. Others contended that rapid environmental shifts ­warmer temperatures, lower salinity levels, and the growth of submerged infrastructures ­had caused a sudden proliferation of endemic teredo populations that had previously gone mostly unnoticed.

The search for a defence

What is certain is that, due to the growing importance of maritime trade and colonial plunder, European states became increasingly attentive to anything that threatened merchant vessels and commercial infrastructures. Growing anxieties about shipworm infestations coincided with unprecedented action against piracy. While legal scholars crafted a new concept of pirates as the “enemies of humanity” and colonial navies patrolled the seas, navies and trading networks mobilised resources to protect their fleets from the devastating incursions of shipworms and sea termites.

Imperial powers conducted extensive botanical research in the hope of finding exotic woods that would prove to be impervious to shipworm infestations. Endless experimentation, however, yielded scarce results. While they found no timber that could completely resist the shipworm’s persistent burrowing, Cuban cedar, South American greenheart, African cabopa and Australian eucalyptus were prized by many shipbuilders as offering some protection. This turned them into valuable colonial resources, making them the target of often unsustainable extractive practices.

The development of a capitalist world economy took its first step in the context of a global mobilisation against sea termites and shipworms.

The search for technological fixes to what maritime scholars Paola Palma and L. N. Santhakumaran refer to as the “global worming” of the ocean continued. In the 1760s, the British Navy invested heavily in the search for metallic covers to protect its fleet. Following the discovery that copper was not only an effective physical barrier, but that it also leeched toxic chemicals that killed shipworm larvae, Britain began to copper-plate all its naval vessels – a practice that was gradually adopted by most European navies, colonial trading companies, and slaving ships.

The hastened metallisation of the world’s fleet had far-reaching socio-ecological impacts, stimulating the expansion of mining operations around the world. Copper mines were excavated deeper below sea level, exposing them to devastating flooding. This, in turn, stimulated the development of early steam engines. Although seldom noticed by historians, the Industrial Revolution ­and thus the development of a capitalist world economy ­took its first step in the context of a global mobilisation against sea termites and shipworms, designed to secure ocean spaces for capital circulation.

Copper sheathing, however, had its limits. The metal protections often cracked, exposing tender wood to shipworms. Only the development of iron ships in the middle of the 19th century finally immunised the shipping industry from shipworm infestations. The Teredo navalis appeared to have been finally relegated to the footnotes of highly specialist maritime historiographies. However, the tension between capital’s tendency to expand into ever-new frontiers and shipworms’ inclination to burrow and hollow persisted, and the crisis soon re-emerged.

A new network under the sea

The laying of the first transoceanic cable between Britain and the United States in 1858 kickstarted a new phase of infrastructure building in the ocean floor. The Atlantic was the first ocean to be crossed by telegraph lines. Soon, colonial agents, electrical engineers and oceanographers were closely collaborating to link France to Algeria, Britain to India, and Portugal to Angola. By the end of the century, telegraph cables ran under all major oceans, facilitating colonial control, logistical operations, and the rapid growth of transnational financial markets.

The worm turns: an 1860 cartoon shows a prominent zoologist being attacked by the object of his studies – a shipworm.

From De Nederlandsche Spectator

Shipworms, however, would soon reveal themselves to be – once again – an inconvenient obstacle, this time to the accelerated circulation of information. In his Statistics of Telegraphy, Sir James Anderson, then General Manager of the Eastern Telegraph Company ­– described “teredo of all kinds” as an “enemy of submarine cables”, lamenting that “cables have been recovered from depths of twelve hundred fathoms with all the hemp eaten away, and the core pitted with these marine animals”.

Boring through and digesting the mix of jute and gutta-percha that cable companies used as insulation, the molluscs exposed the precious copper wires to seawater. The resulting damage was often systemic, requiring long oceanic surveys to locate the ruptures, hoist the cables from the depths and repair them. No longer a major concern for the shipping industry, shipworms had once again burrowed their way into anthropocentric history.

These interspecies conflicts delayed the development of telegraphic imperialism, while stimulating a renewed interest in deep-sea ecologies. Engineers, meanwhile, went back to devising fortifications to arrest­ or at least slow down ­the incessant burrowing of the billions of shipworms drifting across ocean geographies. By the end of the century, spiral-wound Muntz metal tapes were deployed to protect endless miles of wires. The smallest crack, however, was soon exploited by shipworms.

The worm returns

Since then, the burgeoning cable industry has never stopped girdling the world with wires. Cable ships rapidly multiplied, working to expand, repair and update the global media network. Telegraphic cables, coaxial phone cables and fibre-optic cables sank to the bottom following repetitive routes, each remaining operational for a few years, to then rest forgotten in the abyssal depths. This transnational and transhistorical infrastructural mesh slowly merged with the natural landscape, creating new hybrid environments and techno-natural entanglements.

Despite attempts to contain their proliferation, shipworms continue to burrow through marine infrastructures, causing an estimated billion dollars in damages each year.

By 1957, major communication companies reported 14 instances of whales ensnared in submarine cables. In the 1980s, multiple cable failures were blamed on barracuda bites. By 2014, sharks had so often sabotaged fibre-optic networks that Google announced that it would begin casing its cables in purposely designed protective Kevlar bands. And, despite ever-renewed attempts to contain their proliferation, shipworms continue to burrow through marine infrastructures, causing an estimated billion dollars in damages each year.

Global warming has only intensified the gradual worming of the planet, creating ideal environmental conditions for these life forms. We can perceive this warming, worming present as the result of complex interspecies interactions, whose history too often remains hidden and submerged,­ revealing the limits of many anthropocentric histories of capitalism and globalisation. Modernist projects have often strived to turn ocean spaces into an immense hyperspace in which trade and information could flow in predictable and ever-faster ways. Yet, recalcitrant forms of life have thwarted these projects at every turn.

Today, marine biology is gradually shifting towards unprecedented plans to harness and exploit – rather than contain and eradicate – shipworm populations. In Thailand, people have traditionally eaten shipworms. Could including humans in this marine food chain be the unexpected key to winning the rock, paper, scissors game against the shipworm?

In 2023, a research project at the University of Plymouth developed the first pilot system for shipworm aquaculture: rebranding teredinidae as naked clams “rich in nutrients including vitamin B12 and monounsaturated fatty acid”. Genomic studies, moreover, have indicated that symbiotic bacteria living within the teredos’ wormlike bodies release the special enzymes that break down lignin, the substance that provides stiffness to plants, and decompose wood. This has given rise to a host of projects seeking to mass-produce the same bacterial enzymes to convert timber into biofuels. What sort of ocean worlds these cross-species encounters will engender remains to be seen, but it seems clear that the humble shipworm will continue to shape our future, just as it has our past.

An example of the devastation caused by a major shipworm infestation in San Francisco Bay in the 1920s.

San Francisco Bay Marine Piling Committee, 1927
