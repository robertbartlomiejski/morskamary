Ludwik Janiszewski's Theory of Marinisation
Core Theoretical Framework
Definition of Marinisation
In a broad sense, marinisation encompasses the connections, dependencies, and various influences of the marine factor on different spheres of social life. It primarily refers to phenomena and processes related to the maritime economy, but extends to the generation, shaping, and presence of all 'marine' features (elements) in the material and spiritual culture of society 
1
. This includes phenomena of growth and development of these features, and the individual and collective outcomes they produce 
1
. These 'marine' features include ports, shipyards, ships, marine institutions and organizations, and all products of marine-related activity, both physical and symbolic (ideas, beliefs, customs) 
1
.
In a narrower sense, marinisation is understood as the process of transition from a society dominated by elements of land-based culture to one dominated by elements of marine culture 
2
.
The 'Marine Factor' (czynnik morski)
Janiszewski directly links the concept of 'marinisation' to the 'marine factor' and its influence on social life 
3
. This factor comprises two distinct, yet integrated, elements:
Sea as a natural biophysical environment: This refers to the sea or marine coast as a biophysical (including geographical) environment that exerts influence on human behavior 
3
. This influence can be direct physical impact on the human body, psychological impact through sensory organs, the effect of natural marine resources on economic and socio-political activity, and the influence of marine areas on the distribution and mobility of people 
4
.
Sea as an area of human activity: This refers to the sea as a space for diverse human activities aimed at satisfying individual and social needs 
3
. This element is socio-cultural in nature and is considered a decisive influence on society and its transformations 
4
.
Semi-Deterministic Interaction
Janiszewski rejects extreme determinism, which posits that the marine environment dictates human behavior, but embraces a moderate view 
3
. This moderate view acknowledges that the environment creates specific conditions for existence, but their utilization primarily depends on human entrepreneurship, ingenuity, resourcefulness, and the associated value system 
3
. Human activity, in its purposeful production, not only is shaped by the environment but also increasingly influences it, consciously mastering and subordinating it to vital needs 
4
. The fundamental source of marinisation is human activity related to the sea, forming a system of social actions 
4
.
Mechanisms of Marinisation
Marinisation is driven by several social mechanisms:

Mechanism 1: Professional Activity and Human-Sea Interaction
This mechanism stems from the interaction between professional activity related to the sea and the individual 
5
. Work in the maritime economy (e.g., shipping, fishing, port work, shipbuilding) dictates a specific lifestyle, adapting to the unique conditions of marine existence 
5
. It changes social positions and roles, subordinates human relations to the marine element and the vessel, influences work characteristics (e.g., voyage cycles), and shapes production and service organizations 
5
. This process transforms human consciousness, turning 'landlubbers' into experienced 'sea wolves,' creating a distinct social type 
5
.
Mechanism 2: Creation of Maritime Institutions and Organizations
This involves the establishment of various formal maritime institutions and organizations (economic, political, cultural, scientific, sports, recreational) that conduct rational and organized activities on or related to the sea 
5
. These institutions form a more or less cohesive system that satisfies many important individual and collective needs, contributing to the development of human communities by enriching their elements, diversifying social relations, and raising standards 
5
. They also form the basis for diverse social bonds 
5
.
Mechanism 3: Formation of New Social Collectivities
This mechanism involves the creation of new, differentiated forms of social collectivities 
5
. As a result of the sea's influence and human activity related to it, specific goal-oriented groups emerge, organized around this activity 
5
. These include economic organizations of the maritime economy and non-economic associations (social, scientific, artistic) 
5
. Marinisation also transforms territorial communities, especially those living in coastal areas 
5
. With the development of the maritime economy, new forms of work emerge, and territorial communities are transformed 
6
. New categories of maritime professions and specializations are formed, leading to quantitative and qualitative changes in the professional and class-stratum structure of society 
6
.
Mechanism 4: Social Consequences of Maritime Economic Development
This mechanism arises from the social consequences of maritime economic development 
6
. Such development leads to a diversification of individual and collective needs and an increase in living standards due to enhanced opportunities for their satisfaction 
6
. From a sociological perspective, this process is linked to changes in social relations, forms of collective life and behavior, types of social and professional personalities, lifestyles, aspirations, and value systems 
6
. These external changes are accompanied by internal transformations in the consciousness of individuals, groups, and broader communities 
6
.
Aspects and Dimensions of Marinisation
Marinisation is a complex set of phenomena and processes with multiple dimensions:

Historical Aspect
Marinisation emerged early in human socio-economic history, present in all epochs (ancient, medieval, modern) 
7
. It is evidenced by the material and cultural development of many nations, especially coastal ones, involved in marine exploitation, navigation, maritime trade, fishing, and expeditions 
7
. This historical activity shaped their civilizations and cultures profoundly 
7
. In Poland, this process became more pronounced from the 1930s onwards 
7
. The historical time frame in which the process occurs is the indicator of this aspect 
7
.
Spatial Aspect
This relates to the geographical concentration of marine centers like harbors, fishing settlements, ports, shipyards, and urban agglomerations 
7
. The degree of concentration and geographical location of these objects serve as indicators 
7
. Different areas of human activity related to the sea have varying spatial-geographical ranges of influence 
8
. This influence is most pronounced in coastal regions where the main maritime institutions are located, such as Szczecin and the Tricity in Poland 
8
.
Demographic Aspect
This includes phenomena such as the number of people professionally involved with the sea, population density in coastal areas, and migration movements in maritime centers 
7
. An increase in the percentage of the population living in these areas and involved in the maritime economy or other marine institutions, relative to the total population, indicates this aspect 
7
.
Economic Aspect
This concerns the maritime economy and other marine institutions as places of professional work and primary sources of income and wealth for workers and their families 
7
. The percentage of people supported by sea-related work and their material standard of living are indicators of this aspect 
7
.
Social Aspect
This refers to social phenomena emerging from human activity related to the sea, characterized by complexity 
9
. It includes behavior patterns, social relations, social institutions, social groups and categories, territorial communities, and broader social structures 
9
. Quantitative and qualitative metrics used by sociologists are indicators 
9
.
Cultural and Cultural Aspect
The cultural aspect involves the creation of marine elements, systems, and configurations within marine culture through diverse human activity 
9
. This culture includes material values (e.g., tools, equipment) and spiritual values (e.g., rituals, customs, myths, ideologies, knowledge, art) 
9
. Maritime culture, constantly interacting with 'land-based culture,' is an important factor in shaping marine communities 
9
. The cultural aspect of marinisation relates to activities that promote knowledge about the sea, popularize maritime literature, develop marine education, and organize participation in marine culture, primarily through specialized social and cultural institutions 
9
.
Psychosocial Aspect
This relates to phenomena and processes concerning the formation and development of 'marine consciousness,' new mentality and imagination, and personality traits characteristic of marine communities 
9
. Marinisation processes, linked to these phenomena, manifest in the subtle sphere of social life 
9
.
Object of Maritime Sociology
Maritime sociology, as a new scientific subdiscipline, focuses on the issues of marinisation as a theoretical category and a social process 
3
. It aims to understand the 'marine social reality' by analyzing its specific characteristics and the social changes it undergoes 
10
. The field seeks to describe and explain phenomena and processes within this reality, considering both its structure and dynamics 
10
.

In summary, Janiszewski's theory of marinisation provides a comprehensive framework for understanding the profound and multifaceted impact of the sea on human societies. It highlights the interplay between the natural marine environment and human activity, dissecting the process into various mechanisms and dimensions that shape social, economic, and cultural life in coastal regions and beyond. The theory emphasizes that marinisation is a complex, dynamic process that transforms societies from land-oriented to sea-oriented cultures.
