This paper revisits the field of marine, maritime, ocean, blue sociology in light of the conceptual and policy framework of the blue economy and blue economy transition. Contemporary research distinguishes two key vectors that differentiate these approaches methodologically:
• Marinisation: Focuses on the impact of the sea on land – how proximity to the ocean changes the social structures, culture and identity of coastal residents (characteristic of Janiszewski's tradition).
• Maritimisation: Analyses the impact of humans on the sea – deliberate activities involving the territorialisation, exploitation and “cultivation” of the ocean (characteristic of economic and environmental approaches).

Waters, Seas, and Oceans in the Age of the Oceanic Turn

Abstract
This article offers a critical review of the scholarly literature mobilising the concept of performativity of the sea waters in response to the complexity of society’s relations with the multi-layered planetary circulation of water, porosity, transformations from ice-liquid-gas. Since “the ocean is not one”—as articulated by the theme of the UN Ocean Conference in Nice (2024)—each water system (river, coastal sea, sea, global ocean, hydrosphere, i.e. atmospheric water) performs differently (that is, it enacts, acts, carries out, and manifests itself as a subject revealed through action in specific situations) and therefore requires distinct research epistemologies. The article argues that the performativity of water—understood as the dynamic, relational agency of watery matter in interaction with humans, technologies, institutions, and other-than-human actors—constitutes a conceptual tool capable of overcoming instrumental, disciplinary, sectoral, and marketing-driven exploitative narratives.

Drawing on the theoretical scientific marine sociology field and maritime sociology field (human at sea - territorialisation of the sea by professional, recreational, ritual, technological and economical actions, social organisation, maritime institutions, socio-technological and socio-symbolic processes influenced by the sea) theoretical evolution of maritime sociology, from Michael Poole’s sociological systematization of sea-related work to Ludwik Janiszewski’s “maritimization theory” to differentiate performances of marine factor [sea in-land impact spontaneously through climate regulation, planetary seasons' cycle, seasonal navigation conditions, waving, tides, monsuns, streams, flows ....], maritime factor [human at sea]  framing schemes of interpretation, . We argue that while traditional maritime sociology focused primarily on maritime professions, institutions, and maritime and fishery communities, the blue economy introduces new domains of inquiry such as sustainability, circularity, ocean governance, and blue urbanism. The paper suggests that these domains can serve as entry points to extend maritime sociology beyond “people of the sea” toward an integrated sociology of sea societies. We develop a comparative analysis of the theoretical principles underpinning the blue economy and maritime sociology, and we advance the idea of “blue sociology” as a complementary rather than competitive framework. Furthermore, we outline research directions connecting maritime sociology to contemporary challenges including climate change, technological advancement posthuman ocean theory, and social sustainability in marine spaces.
