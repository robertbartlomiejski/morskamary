Our starting point is maritime sociology, particularly its foundations in the work of Ludwik Janiszewski, who began developing his theory of marinisation in the early 1970s. In the time of the Oceanic Turn, his semi-deterministic theoretical framework deserves not only to be revisited but also to be acknowledged as a means of foregrounding the Sustainable Blue Economy. Our contribution extends beyond re-examining this framework; drawing on the EU Sustainable Blue Economy agenda as a case study, we develop a multi-level model that identifies sectors with strong potential to address blue-related social problems. We propose a delimitation of three interrelated "blue-making" concepts: marine, maritime, and oceanic. The first two correspond to the "marine factor" and "human factor" in Janiszewski’s theory, while the "oceanic" domain integrates these into a planetary, hydrosocial context. We argue that "Blue Sociology" serves as a complementary renewal of the discipline, offering critical analytical resources for addressing the distributive justice, participation, and social sustainability blind spots of dominant blue policy agendas.

1. Introduction
Over the past decade, the Blue Economy (and the closely related policy idiom, Blue Growth) has emerged as the guiding principle and symbolic representation of ocean-based development. Promising a synthesis of economic growth, environmental sustainability, and social inclusion, this framework has been institutionalised by the European Union and global bodies. However, the conceptual openness of the Blue Economy often masks unresolved trade-offs and leaves the social mechanisms of "blue" transformation under-specified.

Drawing on the historical and theoretical evolution of maritime sociology—from Michael Poole’s sociological systematization of sea-related work to Ludwik Janiszewski’s "maritimization theory"—we propose that the Blue Economy offers a renewed analytical and normative framework for the sociological study of sea–society relations. While traditional maritime sociology focused primarily on maritime professions ("people of the sea"), institutions, and occupational communities, the Blue Economy introduces new domains of inquiry such as sustainability, circularity, ocean governance, and blue urbanism.

We advance the concept of "Blue Sociology" not as a competitor to maritime sociology, but as a complementary renewal. This framework extends the discipline beyond the study of occupational groups toward an integrated sociology of sea societies, capable of analysing the complex socio-ecological entanglements inherent in offshore energy, biotechnology, and coastal transitions.

2. Theoretical Foundations: From Maritime Sociology to Blue Sociology
To understand the sociological implications of the Blue Economy, we must first ground our analysis in the foundational traditions of maritime sociology. This section reconstructs two critical pillars: the programmatic systematization offered by Michael Poole and the semi-deterministic theory of marinisation developed by Ludwik Janiszewski.

2.1 Maritime Sociology: The Legacy of Systematization and Marinisation
Maritime sociology has long struggled for recognition as a distinct sub-discipline, often fragmented into studies of specific occupational groups (fisheries sociology) or subsumed under broader industrial sociology. However, the works of Poole and Janiszewski provide a coherent theoretical architecture that anticipates many concerns of the contemporary Oceanic Turn.

2.1.1 Michael Poole: Programmatic Systematization
Michael Poole’s contribution lies in his effort to move maritime sociology beyond descriptive ethnographies of "people of the sea". Poole argued for a programmatic systematization, treating the maritime world not merely as a setting for exotic work, but as a distinct sociological field with unique structural properties. He focused on the totality of the maritime environment—the "total institution" of the ship, the specific labour markets of the port, and the oscillation between land and sea—as a lens to understand broader social phenomena. For Blue Sociology, Poole’s legacy underscores the necessity of analysing the organisational and institutional structures that govern blue sectors, from traditional shipping to modern offshore wind farms.

2.1.2 Ludwik Janiszewski: The Theory of Marinisation
Central to our framework is the Theory of Marinisation (teoria marynizacji), developed by Ludwik Janiszewski in post-war Poland. Janiszewski’s approach is characterised as semi-deterministic: it posits that environmental and infrastructural forces orient social possibilities, while social actors and institutions translate those orientations into particular developmental outcomes (Janiszewski, 1967; 1972).

This theoretical stance navigates between structural determinism and voluntarism. It recognises the constraining power of material conditions (the sea as a physical force) while emphasising the transformative capacity of human agency and social organisation. Janiszewski conceptualised marinisation as a process of mutual constitution: the sea shapes societies through material constraints and opportunities (the Marine Factor), while societies shape their relationships with the sea through institutional choices, cultural practices, and technological mediations (the Human Factor).

Janiszewski identified several key mechanisms of sea-society entanglement through which marinisation produces durable social forms. We reinterpret these mechanisms here to analyse contemporary Blue Economy dynamics:

Economic Linkage Mechanisms:
These involve the creation of maritime occupations, firms, and industries that reconfigure local labour markets and economic structures. When fishing fleets, shipping companies, or—in the modern context—offshore wind maintenance hubs are established in a coastal region, they generate employment opportunities and income flows that orient local development toward maritime sectors. These linkages create material interests that shape political coalitions and regional identities (Janiszewski, 1967).

Institutional Embedding Mechanisms:
Marinisation proceeds through the establishment of port authorities, maritime educational institutions, and regulatory agencies that "lock in" maritime developmental pathways. Once substantial institutional infrastructure is created, path dependencies emerge: actors develop vested interests, specialized knowledge accumulates, and the "switching costs" for alternative development strategies increase. Maritime institutions thus become self-reinforcing, reproducing maritime orientations even as specific economic conditions change (Janiszewski, 1991).

Cultural Transmission Mechanisms:
The reproduction of maritime identities occurs through families, workplaces, schools, and community organisations. When local cultural practices celebrate maritime heritage, or when schools incorporate maritime training, maritime identities become embedded in social reproduction. These mechanisms create subjective orientations toward maritime life—a "maritime consciousness"—that complements economic structures.

Spatial Concentration Mechanisms:
This refers to the clustering of maritime activities and infrastructure in specific coastal locations, creating agglomeration effects. Port cities develop specialised service sectors and built environments adapted to maritime functions. This spatial concentration reinforces marinisation by creating dense networks of maritime actors, fostering what we now might term "Blue Urbanism".

2.2 Distinguishing the Domains: Marine, Maritime, and Oceanic
To operationalise Janiszewski’s theory for the 21st century, we propose a precise delimitation of three interrelated "blue-making" concepts. This tripartite model resolves the confusion often found in Blue Economy policy documents:

Marine Sociology (The Marine Factor): Focuses on the biophysical and ecological agency of the sea. Following Janiszewski’s "marine factor", this domain conceptualises oceans and seas not as empty space, but as an omnipotent biophysical foundation. It deals with the material reality of water, tides, biodiversity, and climate forces that constrain and enable human activity.

Maritime Sociology (The Maritime Factor): Focuses on the techno-economic and institutional exploitation of the sea. Here, we deliberately reframe Janiszewski's "human factor" as the "maritime factor". This domain encompasses the "people of the sea", labour regimes, port infrastructure, legal frameworks (UNCLOS, MSP), and the economic machinery of the Blue Economy. It is where human agency actively "terraforms" the sea.

Oceanic Sociology (The Integrative Domain): This is the emergent domain of Blue Sociology. It integrates the marine and maritime within a planetary, post-human, and hydrosocial framework. It analyses the entanglement of the biophysical and the social—where microplastics enter human bloodstreams, and where carbon markets reshape coral reefs. The "Oceanic" perspective allows us to navigate the "estuary" where the marine and maritime collide.

3. Blue Sociology as a Complementary Renewal
We posit that Blue Sociology is not a competitive replacement for maritime sociology but a necessary expansion. Classic maritime sociology provides the tools to analyse the work and communities of the sea (the maritime factor). Blue Sociology expands this to include the governance, justice, and sustainability of the entire ocean-society system (the oceanic domain).

Using the EU’s institutionalised Sustainable Blue Economy framework, Blue Sociology applies these lenses to explain recurrent tensions around:

Distributive Justice: Who benefits from the enclosure of ocean space for offshore wind?

Participation: How are traditional "people of the sea" (fisheries) consulted in Marine Spatial Planning?

Social Sustainability: Beyond ecological metrics, how do blue sectors support the resilience of coastal communities?

By grounding our analysis in the semi-deterministic logic of Janiszewski—recognising that the "Blue Economy" is constrained by the "Marine Factor" (ecology) and shaped by the "Maritime Factor" (institutions)—we can offer a robust, evidence-based critique of current policy, moving beyond hopeful rhetoric toward a rigorous sociology of the sustainable ocean.

The Tripartite Model of Blue Dynamics
We distinguish three domains of "blue-making," each subject to cycles of erosion (De-) and regeneration (Re-).

1. The MARINE Domain (The Biophysical/Ecological Agency)
Based on Janiszewski’s "Marine Factor" (Czynnik Morski) – The sea as an omnipotent force.

Process of Marinisation: The capacity of the hydrosphere (ocean, tides, climate) to shape human life, impose limits, and dictate survival conditions.

De-marinisation (The Crisis):

Definition: The silencing of the ocean’s agency through pollution, acidification, and biodiversity loss. It is the treatment of the sea as a passive resource pit or waste sink ("Aqua Nullius").

Impact on Blue Value: Collapse of natural capital stocks (fish stocks, coral reefs) renders "Blue Growth" physically impossible.

Re-marinisation (The Regenerative Fix):

Definition: Restoring the "Marine Factor" as a decisive actor. This involves Nature-based Solutions (NbS), rewilding coasts, and granting legal personhood to water bodies (Rights of Nature).

Goal: Moving from extraction to stewardship.

2. The MARITIME Domain (The Techno-Economic/Institutional)
Based on Janiszewski’s "Human Factor" (reinterpreted as "Maritime Factor") – Humans terraforming the sea.

Process of Maritimisation: The institutional, economic, and technological colonisation of the sea (ports, fleets, energy grids, laws).

De-maritimisation (The Loss):

Definition: The decline of traditional "People of the Sea." The rusting of old port infrastructure, the gentrification of working waterfronts into luxury housing, and the loss of intergenerational maritime skills (the "knowledge gap").

Impact on Blue Value: Alienation of coastal communities. If locals feel "sea-blindness" and have no economic stake in the ocean, they resist Blue Economy projects (e.g., protesting offshore wind).

Re-maritimisation (The Socio-Economic Revival):

Definition: The Sustainable Blue Economy. Creating new maritime occupations (wind technicians, algae farmers, blue biotech scientists) that provide dignity and livelihood. It involves "locking in" new institutional pathways (Marine Spatial Planning) that prioritise local labour and community benefits.

Goal: Moving from alienation to participation.

3. The OCEANIC Domain (The Ontological/Integrative)
The New "Blue Sociology" Extension – The planetary, hydrosocial entanglement.

Process of Oceanisation: The realisation that human existence is fundamentally wet, fluid, and connected to the planetary hydrosphere.

De-oceanisation (The Severing):

Definition: The cultural and spiritual disconnection from water. Viewing the ocean merely as a "sector" on an Excel sheet rather than a life-support system. The illusion that terrestrial life is separate from the ocean.

Impact on Blue Value: A "soul-less" economy that generates profit but destroys well-being and fails to account for the "transcorporeal" reality (e.g., microplastics in human blood).

Re-oceanisation (The Consciousness Shift):

Definition: The rise of Ocean Literacy and "Blue Citizenship." Acknowledging Transcorporeality: that we are bodies of water interacting with other bodies of water. This is the "Oceanic Turn" in sociology—recognising that social justice and ocean justice are the same thing.

Goal: Moving from separation to entanglement.

Synthesis: Towards "Just-Inclusive-Solidary-Collaborative-Cohabitation"
True Sustainable Blue Growth requires the synchronisation of all three "Re-" processes. The failure of one leads to the collapse of the others.

The Mechanism of Cohabitation:

Just (Justice): Achieved when Re-maritimisation (new jobs) does not occur at the expense of Re-marinisation (ecosystem health). Example: Offshore wind farms that also function as artificial reefs and are co-owned by local fishing cooperatives.

Inclusive (Participation): Achieved when the Maritime Factor (institutions) listens to the Oceanic Domain (cultural values). Example: Marine Spatial Planning that integrates indigenous/local "tacit knowledge" alongside scientific data.

Solidary (Solidarity): Recognising the Transcorporeal Entanglement. If we poison the water (De-marinisation), we poison ourselves (De-oceanisation). Solidarity is not just between humans, but between humans and the water bodies they inhabit.

Collaborative (Governance): A shift from top-down "Blue Growth" to bottom-up "Blue Society."

def add_table(title, ncols, body_rows):
    """
    Creates a table where the first row is a merged title row across all columns.
    All content is contained inside tables (no standalone paragraphs).
    """
    table = doc_new.add_table(rows=1, cols=ncols)
    table.alignment = WD_TABLE_ALIGNMENT.LEFT
    # title row merge
    title_cell = table.rows[0].cells[0]
    if ncols>1:
        for c in table.rows[0].cells[1:]:
            title_cell.merge(c)
    title_cell.text = title
    title_cell.vertical_alignment = WD_CELL_VERTICAL_ALIGNMENT.TOP
    
    # add body rows
    for r in body_rows:
        row_cells = table.add_row().cells
        for j in range(ncols):
            row_cells[j].text = r[j] if j < len(r) else ""
            row_cells[j].vertical_alignment = WD_CELL_VERTICAL_ALIGNMENT.TOP
            # ensure font in all paragraphs
            for p in row_cells[j].paragraphs:
                p.paragraph_format.line_spacing_rule = WD_LINE_SPACING.SINGLE
                for run in p.runs:
                    run.font.name='Times New Roman'
                    run._element.rPr.rFonts.set(qn('w:eastAsia'), 'Times New Roman')
                    run.font.size=Pt(12)
    return table
