This theoretical stance navigates between structural determinism and voluntarism, recognizing both the constraining power of material conditions and the transformative capacity of human agency and social organization.



The theory maps several key **mechanisms of sea-society entanglement** through which maritimization produces durable social forms:



**Economic linkage mechanisms** involve the creation of maritime occupations, firms, and industries that reconfigure local labor markets and economic structures. When fishing fleets, shipping companies, port operations, or shipbuilding facilities are established in a coastal region, they generate employment opportunities, income flows, and economic dependencies that orient local development toward maritime sectors [1]. These economic linkages create material interests in maritime activity that shape political coalitions, investment priorities, and regional identities.



**Institutional embedding mechanisms** involve the establishment of port authorities, maritime educational institutions, fleet management organizations, and regulatory agencies that lock in maritime developmental pathways [1]. Once substantial institutional infrastructure is created to support maritime activity, path dependencies emerge: institutional actors develop vested interests, specialized knowledge accumulates, and switching costs for alternative development strategies increase. Maritime institutions thus become self-reinforcing, reproducing maritime orientations even as specific economic conditions change.



**Cultural transmission mechanisms** involve the reproduction of maritime identities, practices, and orientations through families, workplaces, schools, and community organizations [1], [5]. When children grow up in households where parents work at sea, when schools incorporate maritime education and training, when local cultural practices celebrate maritime heritage, maritime identities become embedded in social reproduction. These cultural mechanisms create subjective orientations toward maritime life that complement economic and institutional structures.



**Spatial concentration mechanisms** involve the clustering of maritime activities, populations, and infrastructure in specific coastal locations, creating agglomeration effects and distinctive maritime urban forms [1]. Port cities develop specialized labor markets, service sectors, and built environments adapted to maritime functions. This spatial concentration reinforces maritimization by creating dense networks of maritime actors and institutions in specific places.



Janiszewski's framework thus conceptualizes maritimization as a **process of mutual constitution**: the sea shapes societies through material constraints and opportunities, while societies shape their relationships with the sea through institutional choices, cultural practices, and technological mediations. This dialectical understanding anticipates contemporary relational ontologies in oceanic turn scholarship while remaining grounded in empirical analysis of specific maritime communities and sectors.
